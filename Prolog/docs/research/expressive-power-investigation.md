## Brandom on "To Consciousness" and Experience of Error

Brandom makes an "absolutely crucial (if seldom acknowledged) distinction" between things being something **for consciousness** (*fur Bewusstsein*) and things being something **to consciousness** (*ihm*, expressed in the dative). What things are *for consciousness* is the explicit propositional content of a judgment -- what is thought or stated. What things are *to consciousness* is "a functional matter of how they are implicitly taken or practically treated by consciousness."

The experience of error works through this distinction. When a subject discovers a material incompatibility in their commitments (the stick looks bent but feels straight), the incompatibility is initially only something *to consciousness* -- grasped in practice, not as an explicit propositional thought. The subject then acts to repair the error by retaining one belief and rejecting the other. In doing so, the subject "practically treats the retained belief as expressing reality (what the stick is *in itself*) and the rejected belief as expressing a mere appearance (what the stick was merely *for consciousness*)."

Crucially: "the subject -- which Hegel terms 'natural consciousness' -- does not need to explicitly deploy philosophical concepts like 'reality,' 'appearance,' or 'representation' in order for what it does in retaining one of the (contextually) materially incompatible dyad of commitments and rejecting the other to be intelligible as practically taking or treating one as presenting how things really are and the other as presenting a mere appearance."

The representational relation between appearance and reality does not need to be known representationally. It is "functionally established through the practical process of resolving errors." This avoids the Bradleyan regress: "The regress is avoided because the representational relation is not required to be something explicitly for consciousness, but only something to consciousness, a matter of how it is implicitly treated in what consciousness does as part of the experience of error."

**Key finding:** The experience of error is broader than what we might colloquially call "making a mistake." It is the process by which the distinction between appearance and reality gets *practically enacted* through commitment revision. The learner does not need to know they are distinguishing appearance from reality; they only need to *do it*.

## Brandom on Expressive Power (what it actually means in his system)

"Expressive power" has a precise technical meaning for Brandom that differs significantly from any colloquial usage. It operates at two levels:

### In *Making It Explicit*: Logic as Semantic Self-Consciousness

Expressive power is the capacity of a vocabulary to make explicit -- in the form of propositionally contentful claims -- the rules, inferential relations, and normative commitments that are otherwise only implicit in practical actions and linguistic use. Logic does not exist to prove a special class of formal truths but to provide this specific expressive power.

> "Logic is the organ of semantic self-consciousness. It brings out into the light of day the practical attitudes that determine the conceptual contents members of a linguistic community are able to express -- putting them in the form of explicit claims, which can be debated, for which reasons can be given and alternatives proposed and assessed."

Conditionals make it possible to *say* that one claim is a consequence of another. Negation makes it possible to *say* that two claims are incompatible. Before these locutions exist, practitioners can *do* these things (treat one claim as following from another, treat two claims as incompatible) but they cannot bring these practices into the game of giving and asking for reasons.

### In *A Spirit of Trust*: Positive Expressive Freedom

Brandom expands the concept into a theory of agency and historical development. When an individual subjects themselves to the constraints of linguistic norms (sacrificing negative freedom from constraint), they gain a "massive explosion of positive expressive freedom" -- the capacity to "formulate an indefinite number of novel claims, and so to entertain an indefinite number of novel intentions, plans, and conjectures."

This positive expressive freedom is defined as "the capacity to undertake new kinds of commitments, new kinds of responsibility, to acknowledge and exercise new kinds of authority, all of which at once express and develop the self-conscious individuals who are the subjects of those new norms."

Brandom also distinguishes ground-level empirical concepts from philosophical metaconcepts. The metaconcepts' "distinct expressive power lies in their ability to articulate the underlying framework that makes ordinary empirical description possible."

Through this lens, "expressive power is the engine of historical and conceptual progress. As our vocabularies break down and are rationally reconstructed (sublated), we generate new vocabularies with greater expressive power, allowing us to explicitly grasp truths that were previously only latent in our practices."

**Key finding:** "Expressive power" is NOT a loose metaphor for energy or capacity. It has a precise technical meaning: the ability to *say* (make explicit as claims) what was previously only *done* (implicit in practice). Using it as a name for the snap's "output" would need to respect this precision. The snap does not produce "expressive power" in Brandom's sense unless it results in new vocabulary that makes previously implicit commitments explicit.

## Brandom's Modal Framework (possibility -> actuality via determinate negation)

### Possibility as Bedrock

"Hegel's principal metaphysical primitive, determinate negation, is intrinsically and essentially a modal notion. The material incompatibility of red with green and circular with triangular is a matter of what can and cannot be combined, what is and is not possible."

"Possibility is conceptually more basic than actuality, in the sense that an immediately given actual experience is intelligible as having the determinate content it does only insofar as it is situated in a space of possibilities structured by relations of compatible and incompatible differences."

### The Asymmetry That Drives Experience

A crucial asymmetry: "It is impossible for one object simultaneously to exhibit materially incompatible properties, while it is only inappropriate for one subject simultaneously to endorse materially incompatible commitments." Objects cannot be both red and green; subjects CAN (and routinely do) hold contradictory beliefs. When the subject discovers they hold incompatible commitments in actuality, this collision forces revision. This asymmetry is the engine of conceptual development.

### Actualization in Practical Agency

Brandom reads Hegel on action as the paradigm of the possible-to-actual transition:

> "Action alters nothing and opposes nothing. It is the pure form of a transition from a state of not being seen to one of being seen, and the content which is brought out into the daylight and displayed is nothing else but what this action already is in itself." (PG 396)

> "Action simply translates an initially implicit being into a being that is made explicit." (PG 401)

The transition has the structure of a **TOTE loop** (Test-Operate-Test-Exit): "Fulfilling a complex intention is a cyclical process of intervention according to a plan aimed at a goal, observation of the results of the intervention, adjustment of the plan, further intervention, further observation of its results, and so on. ... This is the form of processes by which necessity is incorporated into contingent actuality -- that is, an endorsed end is actualized."

Two perspectives on the same process:
- **Prospectively:** "the move from implicit to explicit is one of change. The content of the intention evolves and develops, becoming more definite and determinate under the influence of the actual circumstances."
- **Retrospectively:** "the move from implicit to explicit is one of revelation of the content of an intention that was all along present, albeit in some sense (visible only retrospectively) implicitly."

And: "objective actuality just is the medium of self-expression. In practical agency, expression is actualization. What one is implicitly for oneself becomes explicit as something actual."

> "Consciousness must act merely in order that what it is in itself may become explicit for it.... An individual cannot know what he is until he has made himself a reality through action." (PG 401)

**Key finding:** The possibility-to-actuality movement is not just about error. It is about the self-constituting process of agency: acting in order to discover what one's commitments actually amount to. The TOTE cycle Brandom describes maps structurally onto the ORR cycle. The snap is one phase of a larger process in which possibility becomes actuality through the incorporation of contingency.

## Carspecken on Expression and Energy

Carspecken's treatment converges with Brandom from a different direction -- through the body and somatic experience.

### Expressivism: Potential to Actual

Drawing on Herder, Hegel, and Marx (via Charles Taylor), Carspecken defines human existence as "an expressive movement from implicit potentialities toward their explicit manifestation." A meaningful act "can be viewed as the explication of an implicit, holistic meaning. The act manifests an impetus into a temporal sequence of behaviors."

Crucially, Carspecken insists that "we do not fully know what we intend to express before we express it. Rather, acting expressively is what clarifies and actualizes the initial, implicit notion." This parallels Brandom's Hegel: "An individual cannot know what he is until he has made himself a reality through action."

### Energy and the Feeling-Body

Carspecken describes a process of deep relaxation where meaning accumulates as somatic energy:

> "Successful series of letting go ... result in a building of body sensations, a building of the energies in your feeling-body, to an intolerable potential. At some point you have to act, you cannot let go any longer."

This is the phenomenological version of the snap: energy builds in the feeling-body "to an intolerable potential" and then erupts into action. The transition is not optional -- "at some point you have to act." The accumulated forestructures of meaning "seem to contain more and more possibilities."

### Validity as Recognition

When potential is transformed into actualized expression, its success is judged by "recognition": "what is actualized corresponds to the telos of the impetus that gave rise to it." But Carspecken notes that any single act is usually only a "partial actualization of the original impetus." Self-actualizations "result in contradictions between what was anticipated in the beginning and what is actualized at the end. The experience of contradiction results in a 'notion,' a deeper understanding oriented toward a possible future actualization."

**Key finding:** Carspecken provides the somatic/phenomenological account of what the code models computationally. The tension_dynamics.pl accumulation is a formal flattening of his "building of the energies in your feeling-body, to an intolerable potential." The crisis is the moment when "you have to act, you cannot let go any longer." The difference: Carspecken's account is developmental and involves recognition/contradiction cycles. The code's snap is a single discontinuous event. This is a known productive limitation.

## What the Incompatibility Semantics Actually Does

The sequent calculus in `incompatibility_semantics.pl` implements `proves/4` with signature `(Sequent, ResourcesIn, ResourcesOut, Proof)`. It tracks:

1. **Material incompatibility** (via `is_incoherent/1`): Two propositions are incompatible if they cannot both be true of the same thing. This is Brandom's determinate negation implemented as a Prolog check.

2. **Modal context** (compressive/expansive): Each inference step operates in a modal context that determines its cognitive cost. Compressive context (necessity, cognitive narrowing) costs 2 units per inference. Expansive context (possibility, cognitive opening) costs 1 unit. This models the "embodied experience of reasoning."

3. **PML rhythm axioms** (via `pml_rhythm_axiom/2`): State transitions between modes, where moving from one modal context to another has associated costs and changes the context for subsequent inferences.

4. **Resource exhaustion as perturbation**: When resources drop below cost, `check_viability/2` throws `perturbation(resource_exhaustion)`. This is the code's implementation of the snap -- the point where the system's way of being is proven inadequate.

5. **Arche-Trace erasure** (via `construct_proof/4`): When the sequent contains the Trace entity (`contains_trace/1`), the proof object is replaced with `erasure(RuleName)`. The derivation succeeds structurally but the proof contains no content. This marks where formalization honestly stops.

The prover is an embodied reasoner: it consumes resources, switches modal contexts, and throws perturbations. The key connection to the snap: the incompatibility semantics does not just detect errors -- it models the *cost* of reasoning and the *mode* (compressive vs. expansive) in which reasoning happens. The perturbation is thrown not because the logic fails but because the resources are exhausted -- the system literally cannot afford to continue in its current mode.

This connects to Brandom's asymmetry: objects cannot hold incompatible properties, but the prover CAN hold incompatible commitments (until it runs out of resources to continue). The perturbation forces revision.

## The Snap as Modal Transformation (synthesis)

The snap in the codebase operates at three levels that correspond to different aspects of the Brandomian modal framework:

### Level 1: Tension Dynamics (the physics metaphor)

In `tension_dynamics.pl`, tension accumulates continuously as the system works. The accumulation is modally inflected: compressive context adds 2.5x tension per inference, expansive context adds only 0.3x. When the second derivative of tension goes negative (stability < 0), the system has entered the catastrophe zone. The code comments are explicit: "This maps onto the Zeeman machine: positive stability = the ball sits in a well. Negative stability = the well has become a hill. The ball must roll."

This is the literal transformation of potential energy to kinetic energy that Tio describes. Tension accumulates (potential), the surface tilts (stability goes negative), the ball rolls (perturbation thrown). After crisis resolution, `relax_tension` partially reduces the tension (hysteresis) -- the system remembers the stress but releases the acute pressure.

### Level 2: Execution Handler (the crisis taxonomy)

In `execution_handler.pl`, perturbations are classified into types:
- **tension_instability**: The catastrophe surface has gone unstable
- **efficiency_crisis**: Strategy exists but is too slow
- **unknown_operation**: Operation type never encountered
- **normative_crisis**: Mathematical norms violated
- **incoherence_crisis**: Contradictory commitments

Each type drives a different response: oracle consultation, domain expansion, context shift, or belief revision. The handler is a taxonomy of ways that possibility can fail to become actuality -- and a taxonomy of recovery paths.

### Level 3: Incompatibility Semantics (the justificatory layer)

In `incompatibility_semantics.pl`, the prover tracks modal context and resource consumption. The perturbation emerges from the mismatch between what the system needs to prove and what it can afford. This is Brandom's asymmetry enacted: the system holds commitments that are formally consistent but computationally unsustainable.

### The full cycle as modal transformation

The complete cycle looks like this:

1. **Possibility space**: The system begins with a space of strategies (meaning field), some learned, some available from the oracle. The learner's meaning field is dense with possibilities -- many ways the computation could proceed.

2. **Accumulation**: As computation proceeds, tension accumulates (potential energy builds). The modal context inflects how fast tension builds -- compressive reasoning generates more strain than expansive reasoning.

3. **The snap**: Stability goes negative, or resources exhaust. A perturbation is thrown. This is the discontinuous transition -- the disc jumps between basins in the Zeeman metaphor. It is also the moment where a material incompatibility becomes *to consciousness* in Brandom's sense: the system's practical failure functionally enacts the distinction between "what I was doing" (mere appearance) and "what I should have been doing" (reality, the oracle's strategy).

4. **Recovery**: The system consults the oracle, synthesizes a new strategy, validates it. The meaning field is depopulated: some possibilities are ruled out. Tension partially relaxes (hysteresis). The system retries with new resources.

5. **Retrospective revelation**: From the retrospective view (Brandom's recollection), the crisis reveals what the system's commitments "always already" implicitly amounted to. The new strategy was, retrospectively, always what the system needed -- it just didn't know it yet.

## Naming Implications

### Why "Experience of Error" is not wrong but may be too narrow

"Experience of Error" correctly identifies the Hegelian/Brandomian mechanism: the practical enactment of the appearance/reality distinction through commitment revision. It is the right name for *what happens at the snap point*.

But the snap point is one phase of a larger process that includes:
- The accumulation of tension (possibility space filling up with strain)
- The modal inflection of how fast tension builds (compressive vs. expansive)
- The hysteresis after crisis (the system remembers its stress)
- The retrospective recollection that integrates the crisis into a developmental narrative

"Experience of Error" names the crisis. The broader cycle includes the conditions that make crisis possible and the aftermath that makes it developmental.

### What Brandom's framework suggests

Brandom's broader term for the complete process is **experience** (*Erfahrung*), which he understands as "a multiphase process" involving:
1. Discovery of material incompatibility in commitments
2. Practical revision (retaining one, rejecting the other)
3. Retrospective recollective reconstruction that integrates the revision into a "cumulative, expressively progressive process"

The third phase is what makes experience more than error correction. It is the process of revealing to consciousness what things "have implicitly all along been, in themselves."

### What Carspecken's framework adds

Carspecken's term is **self-actualization through expression**: the movement from implicit potentiality to explicit manifestation, where "the validity of a self-actualization is in the form of recognition: what is actualized corresponds to the telos of the impetus that gave rise to it."

His account adds the somatic dimension missing from Brandom: the building of energies "to an intolerable potential," the inevitability of action ("at some point you have to act"), and the partial character of any single actualization.

### Candidate framings (not recommendations)

1. **Experience of Error** -- the current name. Correct for the snap point. Narrow for the full cycle.

2. **Expressive Actualization** -- Brandom's language for the possibility-to-actuality transition in agency. Risk: "expressive power" has a precise technical meaning in Brandom (making implicit commitments explicit as claims), and the snap does not always produce new vocabulary in that sense.

3. **Modal Transformation** -- descriptively accurate for what the code does (transition between modal contexts under pressure). Risk: too abstract, loses the developmental/phenomenological content.

4. **Determinate Negation** -- Hegel's/Brandom's term for the process by which possibilities are ruled out through material incompatibility. This is what the snap structurally enacts. Risk: requires substantial philosophical context to understand; may sound like "just negation" without it.

5. **Crisis of Accommodation** -- Piagetian language that connects to the math-ed literature. The system's current schemes are inadequate; accommodation (not just assimilation) is required. Risk: loses the modal/logical precision that Brandom's framework provides.

6. **The Snap** -- the colloquial term already in use. Advantage: honest about its metaphorical character, does not overclaim. Disadvantage: does not connect to any philosophical vocabulary.

### The deeper question

The naming question may be less about finding the right word and more about deciding which aspect of the cycle to foreground:

- If the snap is primarily about **the crisis itself** (the moment of instability), "Experience of Error" is appropriate.
- If the snap is primarily about **the accumulation and release** (potential to kinetic energy), a term from catastrophe theory or energetics would be more appropriate.
- If the snap is primarily about **the developmental process** (what the learner becomes through the cycle), Brandom's or Carspecken's expressivist vocabulary (actualization, self-expression, self-constitution) would be more appropriate.
- If the snap is primarily about **the modal structure** (possibility space being depopulated through determinate negation), Brandom's logical vocabulary would be most precise.

The answer likely depends on audience. For math educators: accommodation. For philosophers: determinate negation or experience. For the code: perturbation and recovery. For the More Machine: the snap.
