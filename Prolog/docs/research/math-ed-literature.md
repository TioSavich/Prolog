## Strategy Development as Computation

Papers that model children's arithmetic strategy development as automata, state machines,
computational systems, or executable programs.

### Information-Processing Models and Executable Programs

**Briars & Larkin (1984)** and **Riley, Greeno, & Heller (1983)** developed
information-processing models of how children build representations of arithmetic word
problems. Their explicit goal was to create models in the form of executable computer
programs whose output matches that of human learners. Cobb (1987) analyzed these models
and contrasted them with Steffe et al.'s (1983) constructivist counting schemes, noting
that Steffe's model "cannot be expressed in the precise formalisms of a computer language"
because it relies on Piaget's reflective abstraction -- which cannot be an operation of an
executable computer program.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on automata/computational systems.*

**Bobrow (1968)** developed the STUDENT computer program, which simulated translation of
verbal word problems into mathematical expressions. **Paige & Simon (1966)** used
Bobrow's program as the basis for a model of human cognitive processes in solving verbal
algebra problems.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on automata/computational systems.*

### Buggy Algorithms and Symbol Manipulation

**Brown & Burton (1978)** and **Brown & VanLehn (1982)** modeled children's systematic
errors in subtraction as "buggy algorithms." Brown and VanLehn proposed a generative
theory of bugs where errors arise when children attempt to repair a procedure by relaxing
certain mathematical constraints. The repair process operates on a representation of
subtraction in terms of symbol manipulation, without reference to quantities and their
relationships.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on automata/computational systems.*

### Connectionist and Associative Network Models

**Dallaway (1994)** presented two connectionist models: one of memory for multiplication
facts and another of children's procedural errors in multicolumn multiplication. Both
were built entirely from connectionist components.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on automata/computational systems.*

Multiple researchers validated computer models based on associative networks to simulate
how children develop rapid retrieval of single-digit multiplication facts:
**Stazyk, Ashcraft, & Hamann (1982)**, **Siegler (1988)**, **Ashcraft (1992)**,
**Campbell (1994)**. These models connect operands with products in associative network
structures.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on automata/computational systems.*

### Knowledge States and Tutoring Systems

**Birenbaum, Kelly, & Tatsuoka (1993)** used the "rule-space model" to diagnose students'
mathematical knowledge states, classifying students into states based on solution
approaches and mapping transitional relationships via tree diagrams. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on automata/computational systems.*

**Schulze (1988)** developed PRECEPTOR, a computational tutoring system that explicitly
modeled the cognitive processes and problem-solving skills of students as they transformed
word problems into abstract algebraic equations.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on automata/computational systems.*

### Constructivism Meets Computational Models

**Wachsmuth & Becker (1986)** argued that information processing models and AI extend
Piaget's research questions. The **Lakos Project** (University of Osnabruck) implemented
a cognitive model in a Prolog machine, based on clinical interviews. It modeled individual
children's mathematical knowledge structures, memory nodes, misconceptions, and cognitive
conflicts as logical propositions in indexed memory nodes.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

**Goldin (1998)** observed that cognitive science merged artificial intelligence, heuristic
programming, and computer simulations with the broader epistemological ideas of Piagetian
developmental psychology and constructivism.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

**Gutstein (1991, 1993)** developed AI-driven computational models of learning,
including "A computer model of learning by human tutors" and "SIFT: A self-improving
fractions tutor."
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

### Steffe's Constructivism and Computer Microworlds

**Biddlecomb (1994)** directly connected Steffe's theories to software architecture in
"Theory-based development of computer microworlds" (Journal of Research in Childhood
Education).
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

**Steffe & Wiegel (1994)** explored how digital environments facilitate schema development
in "Cognitive play and mathematical learning in computer microworlds" (Journal for
Research in Childhood Education).
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

**Steffe & Olive (1996)** analyzed computational mediation of learning in "Symbolizing as
a constructive activity in a computer microworld" (Journal of Educational Computing
Research).
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

**Olive (1993, 2000)** examined construction of fractional schemes using interactive
computational tools (TIMA: Sticks and JavaBars) in "Children's construction of fractional
schemes in computer microworlds" and "Computer tools for interactive mathematical
activity in the elementary school" (International Journal of Computers for Mathematical
Learning).
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

**Nabors (2000)** applied Steffe's constructs to computational settings in "Students'
conceptual constructs involved in solving proportional reasoning problems within a
computer microworld environment."
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

### Constructivist Design of Computational Media

**diSessa (1979, 1986)** grounded instructional design in Piagetian constructivism,
outlining principles for designing computational media that mesh with human cognitive
realities. Works include "On 'learnable' representations of knowledge" and "Models of
computation."
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

**Edwards (1990, 1995)** applied constructivist principles to computational models in
"The role of microworlds in the construction of conceptual entities" and "The design and
analysis of a mathematical microworld" (Journal for Educational Computing Research).
*Source: JMB notebook, query on Piaget/Steffe constructivism and computational models.*

## Crisis-Driven Learning / Perturbation

Papers where cognitive breakdown, error, or contradiction triggers reorganization of
mathematical knowledge.

### Perturbation and Scheme Reorganization (Constructivist Tradition)

**von Glasersfeld (1995)** summarized Piaget's equilibration theory: cognitive change
occurs when a mental scheme fails to produce an expected result, leading to a perturbation
that forces an "accommodation" reorganizing the scheme to reestablish equilibrium.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

**Hackenberg (2010)** detailed the micro-level experience of perturbation: when a person
fails to achieve an intended goal, it creates a "disturbed state of affairs" often
accompanied by emotions like disappointment or surprise. If the student can weather the
emotional depletion, they modify their operations to eliminate the crisis and build a more
powerful scheme. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

**Norton (2008)** and **Steffe (1991)** described **metamorphic accommodation** as the
most profound type of crisis-driven reorganization: driven by a "recursive and persistent
perturbation" that projects operations from a lower plane of knowledge to a completely
new, higher plane, vertically reorganizing the entire cognitive system. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

**Simon, Tzur, Heinz, & Kinzel (2004)** discussed engineering "cognitive conflict" by
posing tasks that contradict a learner's current conceptions, warning that this only
triggers reorganization if the student actually recognizes and is convinced by the
contradiction. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

### Piaget's Perturbation Framework (JMB sources)

**Piaget (1985)** defined perturbations as anything that creates an obstacle to
assimilation or prevents achieving a goal: "If perturbations are defined as anything
creating obstacle to assimilation or to achieving a goal, all regulations are, from the
subject's point of view, reactions to perturbations."
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

**von Glasersfeld (1980, 1995)** modeled learning through scheme theory (recognition
template, activity, expected result). When the result of goal-directed activity is
unexpected, resolving the perturbation is the impetus for establishing a new scheme.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

**Nabors (2003)** tracked students' mathematical expansion by analyzing specific
"perturbations encountered and the accommodations made" in fractional tasks within
computer microworlds. Published in Journal of Mathematical Behavior.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

**Hohensee (2016)** noted that unexpected or disappointing outcomes can prompt students
to notice new characteristics and accommodate existing schemes. Published in Journal of
Mathematical Behavior.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

### The Disequilibrium Debate

**Harel & Koichu (2010)** defined learning as a "continuum of disequilibrium-equilibrium
phases" driven by intellectual needs (the DNR framework).
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

**Simon (2013)** explicitly challenged this view, arguing that "disequilibrium is neither
necessary nor sufficient to explain mathematics conceptual learning." He provided
empirical examples of students constructing new concepts through carefully sequenced tasks
without encountering obvious obstacles. Published in Journal of Mathematical Behavior.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

Simon noted that tasks can induce perturbations, but learners often cannot resolve them
immediately, making perturbation an incomplete mechanism for explaining how learning
actually occurs. Steffe (1991) called such transitions "metamorphic accommodation" where
"the researcher can conclude that learning took place, but there is a lack of data on the
learning process."
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

### Errors as Springboards for Inquiry

**Borasi (1994)** advocated capitalizing on errors as "springboards for inquiry," relying
on **Lakatos (1976)** who demonstrated that mathematical knowledge historically grows
through a dialectic of "proofs and refutations." The doubt and cognitive dissonance
generated by an error can force students to rethink the foundations of their mathematical
definitions. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

### Conflict Teaching

**Tirosh & Graeber (1990)** deployed a "conflict teaching approach" (citing Swan, 1983) to
deliberately evoke cognitive conflict in preservice teachers, forcing them to face
inconsistencies between flawed intuitions and actual calculation results. Published in
JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

**Bell (1988)** discussed the systematic use of cognitive conflict in teaching.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

**Verschaffel & De Corte (1997)** designed nonroutine, problematic word problems
specifically to elicit cognitive conflict, inducing revision of stereotypical beliefs about
school mathematics. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

### Epistemological Obstacles

**Bishop et al. (2014)** framed breakdowns as "epistemological obstacles" drawing on
**Bachelard (1938/2002)** and **Cornu (1991)**. An obstacle occurs when knowledge useful
in one context (e.g., whole numbers) leads to contradictions in a new context (e.g.,
negative integers), compelling the learner to modify their thinking. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

**Tall & Schwarzenberger (1978)** detailed specific conflicts that arise when students
learn about real numbers and limits -- profound epistemological obstacles. Published in
Mathematics Teaching.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

### Contradictions and Expansive Transformations (Activity Theory)

**Tomaz & David (2015)** applied Engestrom's (2001) activity theory to examine how
introducing everyday situations into traditional classrooms creates structural tensions
and "contradictions." These are not mere conflicts but historically accumulated structural
tensions that cause "ruptures." When overcome, breakdown triggers an "expansive
transformation" -- a radical reconceptualization of the entire mathematical activity
system. Published in JRME.
*Source: JRME notebook, query on crisis-driven learning.*

### Commognitive Conflict

**Rabin, Hershkowitz, & Schwarz (2013)** utilized Sfard's (2007) concept of "commognitive
conflict" -- where communication is hindered because different discursants act according
to different meta-rules. Unlike factual disagreements resolvable by proof, commognitive
conflicts require the student to gradually accept the teacher's new discourse conventions
before understanding their "inner logic." Published in Journal of Mathematical Behavior.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

### Cognitive Conflict and Proof

**Hadas, Hershkowitz, & Schwarz (2000)** described how introducing contradiction and
uncertainty in dynamic geometry environments promotes students' need to seek mathematical
proofs. Published in Educational Studies in Mathematics.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

**Zaslavsky (2005)** focused on instructional benefits of intentionally creating
uncertainty in learning mathematics. Published in Educational Studies in Mathematics.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

### Conflict with Institutional Norms

**Alacaci & Pasztor (2002)** explored the tension students experience when personal
mathematical sense-making conflicts with expectations of flawed standardized test items.
Published in Journal of Mathematical Behavior.
*Source: JMB notebook, query on crisis/perturbation/disequilibrium.*

## Fractions as Continuous Development

Papers arguing that fraction understanding develops continuously from whole-number
understanding rather than requiring a categorical leap.

### Siegler's Integrated Theory

**Siegler, Thompson, & Schneider (2011)** proposed an "integrated theory of whole number
and fractions development," arguing the common core of numerical development is magnitude
knowledge. Both natural and non-natural numbers possess magnitudes and can be placed on
the same number line. Substantial effort should be placed on helping students recognize
these deep similarities. Published in Cognitive Psychology.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on fractions as continuous development.*

### Predictive Links from Early Childhood

**Bailey, Siegler, & Geary (2014)** and **Jordan et al. (2013)** showed that children's
conceptual knowledge of natural numbers and number sequence skills at age six reliably
predict conceptual knowledge of fractions in early adolescence. If a categorical leap were
strictly necessary, one might expect early proficiency to be uncorrelated or negatively
correlated with later fraction success. Published in Developmental Science and other
journals.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on fractions as continuous development.*

### Spontaneous Focusing on Numerosity (SFON)

**McMullen, Hannula-Sormunen, & Lehtinen (2015)** tracked children from age 6 to 12 and
found that high SFON tendency before school age strongly predicts advanced rational number
conceptual knowledge later on. They hypothesize that children who frequently practice
whole-number counting in everyday situations encounter scenarios where natural numbers are
insufficient (e.g., sharing two cookies among three friends), supporting a "smoother and
more consistent transition from natural to rational number concepts" rather than a
disjointed leap. Published in ZDM Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on fractions as continuous development.*

### Instructional Bridging via the Number Line

**Saxe, Diakow, & Gearhart (2012)** designed the Learning Mathematics through
Representations (LMR) curriculum, using the number line as a continuous, cross-domain
representational context. Starting with integers and using the same definitions (unit
interval, order) to introduce fractions, the curriculum leverages students' whole-number
intuitions about linear magnitude to build fraction understanding without requiring a
conceptual rupture. Published in ZDM Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on fractions as continuous development.*

### Bridging Analogies and Cross-Domain Mapping

**Vamvakoussi (2017)** noted the importance of "cross-domain mapping" and "bridging
analogies" -- such as viewing the number line as a continuous "rubber band" -- to help
students see how discrete counting numbers map onto the continuous, infinitely divisible
space of fractions. Published in ZDM Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on fractions as continuous development.*

### The Conceptual Change Counter-Position

For context, several researchers argue the transition does require conceptual change:
**Vamvakoussi & Vosniadou (2004, 2010)**, **Vosniadou & Verschaffel (2004)**,
**Christou (various)** on natural number bias. The framework theory approach (Vosniadou
et al., 2008) holds that children's structured framework theories of number based on
natural number features must be radically restructured for rational numbers.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on fractions as continuous development.*

## Interactive Tools for Strategy Visualization

### Computer-Guided Clinical Interviews

**Ginsburg et al. (2016)** described the **mCLASS:Math system**, a computer-guided
clinical interview tool for teachers. The software guides teachers through assessing
children's solutions to addition, subtraction, and multiplication problems. It provides
an interface to record "observed strategies" (counting on fingers, using chips, derived
facts) and generates automated reports plotting strategy frequency and evolution over
time. In 2014, the system was used to assess over 300,000 students. Published in ZDM
Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on interactive tools.*

### Adaptive Strategy Games

**Lehtinen et al. (2017)** discussed the **Number Navigation Game (NNG)**, a computer
application fostering flexible and adaptive arithmetic strategies. Rather than presenting
ready-defined tasks, the game requires players to create their own calculation strategies
to navigate through increasingly demanding numerical constraints. Randomized experiments
showed improvement in adaptive number knowledge and transfer to pre-algebra skills when
accompanied by well-planned teacher support. Published in ZDM Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on interactive tools.*

### Virtual Environments for Subtraction

**Broza & Kali Kolikant (2015)** detailed an **ice-cream shop computer game** for
low-achieving students. Students take orders, calculate prices, and give change.
Subtraction strategies emerge spontaneously, and students gradually transition from
concrete monetary representations to formal mathematical symbols. Published in ZDM
Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on interactive tools.*

### Digital Mathematical Machines

**Maschietto & Soury-Lavergne (2013)** developed the **e-pascaline**, a digital simulation
of a historical mechanical arithmetic machine. Students interact with it to perform
arithmetical operations and explore decomposition procedures based on place value.
Published in ZDM Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on interactive tools.*

### Diagnostic Assessment Systems

**Confrey & Maloney (2015)** reported on an **interactive diagnostic assessment system
(IDAS)**, a tablet-based tool evaluating students' equipartitioning strategies (fair
sharing). The software records student strategies in real time and provides rapid, precise
feedback to teachers about where a child is on the learning trajectory. Published in ZDM
Mathematics Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on interactive tools.*

### Digital Interventions for Dyscalculia

**Butterworth & Laurillard (2010)** reviewed digital interventions for dyscalculia and low
numeracy, including their own interactive programs for dot pattern matching and number
lines. They highlighted **The Number Race** (Wilson & Dehaene), an adaptive digital game
strengthening children's foundational sense of number. Published in ZDM Mathematics
Education.
*Source: ZDM notebook, query on interactive tools.*

### Simulation Assessments for Teachers

**Shaughnessy, Boerst, & colleagues (2018)** described a **simulation assessment** using
a standardized "student" (teacher educator trained to act according to a specific child
reasoning profile). Prospective teachers interact with this standardized student to
practice eliciting and probing student thinking. Published in JMTE.
*Source: JMTE notebook, query on tools for teacher understanding.*

### Animated Simulations

**Charalambous, Hill, & Ball (2011)** and **Charalambous (2015)** utilized an **animated
simulation** featuring a cartoon teacher delivering a fraction division lesson. Prospective
teachers assumed the role of the teacher to respond to animated students' mathematical
struggles. Published in JMTE.
*Source: JMTE notebook, query on tools for teacher understanding.*

**Estapa, Amador, Kosko, Weston, de Araujo, & Aming'a (2018)** detailed a process where
prospective teachers created their own digital animations as an "approximation of
practice." After watching classroom video, teachers used animation software to recreate
pivotal moments, articulating their professional noticing of children's mathematical
thinking. Published in JMTE.
*Source: JMTE notebook, query on tools for teacher understanding.*

### Virtual Manipulatives for Teacher Development

**Hansen, Mavrikis, & Geraniou (2016)** described **Fractions Lab**, an online virtual
manipulative. Teachers engaged in co-design, which challenged their technological,
pedagogical, and content knowledge. Published in JMTE.
*Source: JMTE notebook, query on tools for teacher understanding.*

### Computer Microworlds for Teacher Model-Building

**Norton & McCloskey (2008)** and **Norton, McCloskey, & Hudson (2011)** discussed
**Chopsticks (TIMA: Sticks)**, a computer microworld where children carry out actions on
virtual fraction sticks. Teachers observed students interacting with the software to build
psychological models of students' mathematical operations. Published in JMTE.
*Source: JMTE notebook, query on tools for teacher understanding.*

### Video Analysis Tools

**van Es & Sherin (2002)** designed the **Video Analysis Support Tool (VAST)** to scaffold
teachers analyzing children's mathematical thinking in classroom video clips.
*Source: JMTE notebook, query on tools for teacher understanding.*
