# Primary-Source Vocabulary for Naming the Crisis-Recovery Cycle

Research compiled 2026-04-04 from NotebookLM queries against primary sources.

The code currently calls the crisis-recovery cycle "ORR" (Observe, React, Reorganize).
This name is not grounded in any primary source. The purpose of this document is to
catalog what Brandom, Carspecken, and Hegel actually call the structures the code models,
and to recommend grounded naming.

---

## Brandom's Vocabulary

### The Experience of Error (*Erfahrung*)

Brandom's primary term for the crisis-recovery cycle is **the experience of error**,
which he takes directly from Hegel's concept of *Erfahrung*. He defines it as a
three-phase process:

> "Each episode of experience is initiated by the detection of an anomaly. The subject
> finds herself with commitments that are, by her own lights, materially incompatible.
> Their conceptual contents are such that commitment to one precludes rational
> entitlement to the other. The next phase is repair. This is what one is normatively
> obliged to do, the changes one is responsible for making, in response to the detection
> of incompatible commitments. [...] The final, recollective phase of experience requires
> the subject to engage in a particular kind of rational reconstruction of the course of
> experience that led up to the commitments resulting from the reparative phase.
> Recollection rationalizes a course of experience by retrospectively redescribing it so
> as to exhibit it as expressively progressive."
> -- *A Spirit of Trust*, Ch. on Conceptual Idealism (Erinnerung discussion)

The three phases are named precisely:

1. **Acknowledgment / Registration** -- "the detection of an anomaly," "acknowledgment
   of the material incompatibility of some commitments the subject has made"
2. **Rectification / Repair** -- "repair the acknowledged incompatibility by revising or
   rejecting some of the offending commitments"
3. **Recollection (*Erinnerung*)** -- "retrospective rational reconstruction [...] that
   exhibits it as expressively progressive"

Brandom summarizes all three in a single sentence:

> "critical registration of an incompatibility of commitments, constructive repair of
> the incompatibility by alteration of commitments, and recollective vindication of the
> new constellation of commitments"
> -- *A Spirit of Trust*

### The TOTE Cycle

Brandom also maps the experience of error onto the cybernetic **Test-Operate-Test-Exit
(TOTE) cycle**:

> "the process Hegel calls 'Erfahrung' in general has the structure of a
> Test-Operate-Test-Exit cycle of action and cognition."
> -- *A Spirit of Trust*, discussion of Reason

The mapping: "test" = registering materially incompatible commitments; "operate" =
repairing them by modifying commitments or inferential concepts; cycle continues until
incompatibility is resolved.

### Doxastic / Discursive Updating

Brandom also characterizes the cycle as **doxastic updating** or **discursive updating**:

> "This little parable of one cycle of practical operating and testing involves
> discursive updating of three basic types: expanding one's observations by drawing
> commitment- and entitlement-preserving inferential conclusions; registering any
> resulting materially incompatible commitments; and repairing them by modifying or
> relinquishing some of those commitments, or the concepts that link them
> inferentially."
> -- *Between Saying and Doing*, Lecture 6

### Pragmatic Expressive Bootstrapping

For the specific case where a practice breaks down and is reconstructed at a *higher
level of expressive power*, Brandom uses **pragmatic expressive bootstrapping**:

> "Algorithmic elaboration's limitations necessitate dialectical ruptures requiring
> conceptual change. [...] Pragmatic expressive bootstrapping provides the model:
> systems of practices generate new vocabularies making their implicit normative
> structures explicit."
> -- synthesized from *Between Saying and Doing*, Lecture 2 (via project source)

### Explicitation (Implicit to Explicit)

The broader transition from implicit practical mastery to explicit conceptual
understanding is called **explicitation** -- "making explicit what is implicit":

> "the process of expression in the more complex and interesting cases [is] a matter
> not of transforming what is inner into what is outer but of making explicit what is
> implicit. This can be understood in a pragmatist sense of turning something we can
> initially only do into something we can say: codifying some sort of knowing how in
> the form of a knowing that."
> -- *Making It Explicit*, Introduction; also *Articulating Reasons*, Ch. 1

The formal relation is the **LX relation**: a vocabulary is LX when it is both
algorithmically **elaborated** from and **explicative** of prior inferential practices.

## Carspecken's Vocabulary

### Impetus, Monitoring, and the Vanishing Point

Carspecken does not have a single term for a crisis-recovery cycle because his model
is continuous rather than episodic. Acts evolve through a dynamic of:

1. **Impetus** -- the preconceptual, holistic urge that initiates action. "An impetus
   to act is experienced by the first person, the actor, much as the forestructure of
   the impression of meaning is experienced by the one trying to articulate the point
   of an act." (*Four Scenes*, Sec. 7)

2. **Monitoring** -- the continuous process by which the actor takes second- and
   third-person perspectives on their own act during and after its manifestation.
   Critically, monitoring is not detached observation; it is itself:
   > "the eruption of new impeti to act that are differentiated from an original
   > impetus. Monitoring often begins while an impetus is in the process of being
   > acted out or mentally rehearsed."
   > -- *Four Scenes*, Sec. 9.2

3. **Chained impeti** -- the cascade of new action urges generated by monitoring:
   > "These new impeti are often chained. One can, for example, experience the sort
   > of impetus to act back that the person addressed by one's act may feel and then,
   > in chained formation, experience an impetus to respond to that anticipated
   > response."
   > -- *Four Scenes*, Sec. 9.2

4. **The vanishing point** -- the meaning of the act is never finally explicated; each
   clarification produces new forestructures and new impeti. "The point of an act is a
   vanishing point for the actor; never fully and finally explicable as such through
   acting." (*Four Scenes*, Sec. 15)

### Breakdown Vocabulary

When an act fails, Carspecken's terms are:

- **Falling short** -- "Expressions can fall short of what is anticipated" (*Four
  Scenes*, Sec. 3)
- **Mismatch** -- between the holistic impetus and the impression the completed act
  delivers
- **Unsuccessful communicative act** -- an "incompetently 'filled' form"
- **Frustrated expectation** -- when an audience fails to grasp backgrounded meaning

### Recovery Vocabulary

- **Mending / Repairing** -- everyday recovery from communicative breakdown:
  "actors attempt to 'mend' or 'repair' the disagreement so they can continue to
  coordinate their activities" (*Four Scenes*, via Habermas)
- **Monitoring and remedying** -- "Your ability to monitor your act allowed you to
  experience it as others would, notice that the wrong word was used, and quickly act
  again to remedy the situation" (*Four Scenes*, Sec. 9.1)
- **Reflection / Internalized position-taking** -- stepping back to repair a breakdown:
  "Reflecting is basically an internal shift of position, so that a former state could
  in principle be talked about with others." (*Four Scenes*, Sec. on Reflection)
- **Internal evolution** -- the process by which an impetus evolves internally through
  thought rather than external action. "Thought is the 'internal evolution' of impeti
  through internalized forms of expressive action and monitoring." (*Four Scenes*,
  Sec. 9; draws on McNeill 1992:40)

### Reconstruction (Methodological)

Carspecken reserves **"reconstruction"** and **"reconstructive analysis"** for the
researcher's methodological work of articulating the tacit validity claims that
constitute the horizon of subjects' meaningful acts. This is *not* the actor's own
recovery process.

## Hegel's Vocabulary

### The Three Moments

Hegel's cycle of position, negation, and sublation is described through three "sides"
or moments of every logical concept (*Encyclopedia Logic*, Sec. 79-82):

1. **The Abstract / Understanding (*Verstand*)** -- initial positing (*Setzen*) of a
   concept as fixed and self-identical. "The understanding determines, and holds the
   determination fixed." (*Science of Logic*, Preface)

2. **The Dialectical / Negatively Rational** -- the fixed determinations of the
   understanding contradict themselves and dissolve. The key term here is **determinate
   negation** (*bestimmte Negation*):
   > "negation is equally positive, or [...] what is self-contradictory does not
   > resolve itself into a nullity, into abstract nothingness, but essentially only
   > into the negation of its particular content; or [...] such a negation is not just
   > negation, but is the negation of the determined fact which is resolved, and is
   > therefore determinate negation; [...] in the result there is therefore contained
   > in essence that from which the result derives"
   > -- *Science of Logic*, Introduction (GW 21.38)

3. **The Speculative / Positively Rational (*Vernunft*)** -- sublation. "The speculative
   or the positively rational grasps the unity of the determinations in their
   opposition, the affirmative that is contained in their dissolution and their passing
   over into something else." (*Encyclopedia Logic*, Sec. 82)

### Sublation (*Aufhebung*)

> "The German 'aufheben' ('to sublate' in English) has a twofold meaning in the
> language: it equally means 'to keep,' 'to preserve,' and 'to cause to cease,' 'to
> put an end to.' [...] Something is sublated only in so far as it has entered into
> unity with its opposite."
> -- *Science of Logic*, Book I (GW 21.94-95)

### Negation of the Negation

> "the insight that the negation of negation is something positive appears a trivial
> matter [...] even though its correctness is undeniable -- and not just its
> correctness, but also, on account of the universality of the determinations involved,
> its infinite extension and universal applicability"
> -- *Science of Logic*, Introduction (GW 21.90)

### Absolute Negativity

> "the negativity just considered constitutes the turning point of the movement of the
> concept. It is the simple point of the negative self-reference, the innermost source
> of all activity [*Tatigkeit*], of living and spiritual self-movement
> [*Selbstbewegung*]; it is the dialectical soul which everything true possesses and
> through which alone it is true"
> -- *Science of Logic*, Book III (GW 12.246)

## Naming Candidates for the Crisis-Recovery Cycle

| Candidate | Source | Three-Phase Map | What It Captures | What It Misses |
|-----------|--------|----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Experience of Error** (*Erfahrung*) | Brandom / Hegel | Registration -> Repair -> Recollection | The full cycle including retrospective reconstruction; grounded in both primary sources | "Error" may sound too narrow for crisis types beyond logical incompatibility; the code has no recollection phase |
| **Acknowledgment-Repair-Recollection** | Brandom (*A Spirit of Trust*) | Direct mapping | Exact phase names from the source text | Cumbersome as a cycle name; recollection not implemented in code |
| **TOTE Cycle** | Brandom via cybernetics (Miller/Galanter/Pribram) | Test -> Operate -> Test -> Exit | Computational; maps cleanly to code (test=solve, operate=crisis-handler, exit=success) | Not philosophically distinctive; loses the normative dimension |
| **Doxastic Updating** | Brandom (*Between Saying and Doing*) | Expand -> Register -> Repair | Captures the incremental commitment-revision process | Too generic; "doxastic" is jargon without payoff for the code |
| **Pragmatic Expressive Bootstrapping** | Brandom (*Between Saying and Doing*) | Implicit practice -> Breakdown -> New expressive vocabulary | Captures the specific case where recovery produces higher-level strategy | Only covers the synthesis case, not oracle-backed recovery |
| **Impetus-Monitoring-Chaining** | Carspecken (*Four Scenes*) | Impetus -> Monitoring (falling short) -> New impeti | Captures the continuous, non-episodic character of the process | No single term; the code *is* episodic, not continuous |
| **Falling Short / Mending** | Carspecken (*Four Scenes*) | Act falls short -> Mending/Remedying -> New forestructure | Phenomenologically rich; captures the felt quality of crisis | Too informal for code naming; no single compound term |
| **Sublation** (*Aufhebung*) | Hegel (*Science of Logic*) | Position -> Determinate Negation -> Preservation+Elevation | The most philosophically precise term for what the cycle does when it works | Overloaded; claims too much for what the code actually achieves |
| **Determinate Negation** | Hegel (*Science of Logic*) | A determination negates *itself* specifically, producing content | Captures why crisis is productive, not destructive | Names only the negation moment, not the full cycle |

## Recommendation

### Primary name: **Experience of Error** (Brandom's *Erfahrung*)

This is the strongest candidate for the cycle the code models. Reasons:

1. **It names the exact structure the code implements.** The meta-interpreter attempts
   computation (applying current commitments), the execution handler classifies the
   crisis (registering incompatibility), and the oracle/synthesis engine repairs the
   breakdown (rectification). This is Brandom's three-phase structure with the
   recollection phase honestly absent.

2. **It is grounded in two primary sources simultaneously.** Brandom takes the term
   directly from Hegel's *Erfahrung*, so using it connects the code to both the
   inferentialist framework and the dialectical framework without conflating them.

3. **It does not overclaim.** "Experience of error" is descriptive: the system
   experiences error (literally -- the meta-interpreter fails) and responds. Unlike
   "sublation" or "Aufhebung," it does not claim that the code achieves dialectical
   transcendence.

4. **The missing third phase is productive.** The code has no recollection phase --
   there is no retrospective rational reconstruction. Naming the cycle after Brandom's
   three-phase structure makes the absence of recollection visible as a design gap
   rather than hiding it behind a neutral acronym.

### Phase names (renaming from Observe/React/Reorganize):

| Current | Proposed | Brandom's Term | Code Location |
|---------|----------|---------------|---------------|
| Observe | **Register** | "registration of an anomaly" / "acknowledgment of material incompatibility" | `execution_handler.pl` crisis classification |
| React | **Repair** | "rectification" / "repair the acknowledged incompatibility" | `oracle_server.pl` + `fsm_synthesis_engine.pl` |
| Reorganize | **Recollect** (when implemented) | "recollection" / "retrospective rational reconstruction" | *Not yet implemented* |

### Secondary vocabulary (for documentation, not code names):

- Use **"crisis"** for what the code calls crisis (resource exhaustion, unknown
  operation, normative violation, incoherence). This maps to Brandom's "detection of
  an anomaly."
- Use **"doxastic updating"** when describing what the strategy-set changes represent
  (the system's "beliefs" about what strategies work are being updated).
- Use **"pragmatic expressive bootstrapping"** specifically for synthesis-based recovery
  (where the system builds new strategies from ENS primitives), as distinct from
  oracle-backed recovery (where the system receives strategies from the teacher).
- Reserve **"sublation"** for the manuscript discussion of what the cycle *would* need
  to be if it were philosophically adequate. The code does not sublate; it repairs.

### What not to use:

- **"ORR cycle"** -- ungrounded; drop it.
- **"Aufhebung"** as a code-level term -- overclaims what the code does.
- **"TOTE cycle"** -- loses the philosophical content; if you want a cybernetic framing,
  Brandom already provides the mapping.
- Carspecken's vocabulary as code-level naming -- his model is continuous and
  phenomenological; the code is episodic and computational. The productive tension
  between the two is research content, not naming content. Use Carspecken's terms when
  discussing where the code gets the phenomenology wrong.
