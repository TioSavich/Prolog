## Steffe's Fraction Trajectory

Steffe's account of fraction development is organized around **schemes** -- Piaget's
repeatable, generalized actions, as interpreted by von Glasersfeld. A scheme has three
parts: an activating situation, the child's activity or procedure, and the result of
that activity. Steffe and Olive identify three key fraction schemes, each building on
its predecessor:

1. **Equi-partitioning scheme.** The child can mentally partition a continuous quantity
   into equal parts *before* operating on it (Olive & Steffe, 2002; Steffe, 2002).
   This is the entry point: without equi-partitioning, the child cannot create the
   fractional unit.

2. **Partitive unit fractional scheme.** The child establishes a unit fraction as one
   part of a whole consisting of equal parts, and understands that any one part can
   be *iterated* to re-establish the whole or a sub-part of the whole. "One-eighth
   iterated three times produces three-eighths of the whole" (Steffe, 2002; Tzur, 1999).

3. **Iterative fraction scheme.** The child uses an iterable unit fraction to construct
   fractions *greater than the whole* -- improper fractions. This requires
   simultaneously coordinating the unit fraction, the composite result, and the
   referent whole (Olive & Steffe, 2002).

### The Reorganization Hypothesis

The central theoretical claim is Steffe and Olive's **reorganization hypothesis**: that
children construct their fractional knowledge through reorganization of their existing
number sequences, not by learning an entirely new kind of number from scratch.

Olive (1999) -- "From fractions to rational numbers of arithmetic: A reorganization
hypothesis" -- and Olive & Steffe (2001) demonstrate this through the case of Joe, who
constructed a **connected number sequence (CNS)** by applying his explicitly nested
number sequence (ENS) in the context of continuous quantities. The ENS, which supports
whole-number multiplication through nested unit coordination, gets reorganized when the
child incorporates a notion of *unit length* into the abstract unit items. The
resulting CNS is a bridge structure between counting and fractions.

Steffe (2004) presents learning trajectories for two children, Jason and Laura,
showing how their different partitioning schemes (equi-partitioning vs. simultaneous
partitioning) led to different pathways toward commensurate fractions. Jason could
independently produce recursive partitioning operations; Laura depended on Jason's
explanations to engage in analogous actions.

The reorganization hypothesis directly addresses the continuity question: fractions are
not a categorical leap but a *reorganization* of counting-based structures when those
structures are applied to continuous quantities.

### The Splitting Operation

Norton and Wilkins (2012) model the developmental structure as **the splitting group**
-- a structure isomorphic to the positive rational numbers under multiplication. Their
quantitative analysis of 58 eighth-grade students confirms that the splitting operation
is a necessary precursor to students' constructions of three-level unit coordination.
This affirms and refines an existing hypothetical learning trajectory for advanced
fraction schemes.

## Hackenberg's Unit Coordination

Hackenberg (2007) studied four sixth-grade students over a year-long constructivist
teaching experiment, focusing on the relationship between students' **multiplicative
structures** and their construction of improper fractions. Her central construct is
**units coordination** -- the number of levels of units-within-units that a student can
take as given prior to activity (i.e., that they have *interiorized*).

### Three Levels of Units

The levels, illustrated through the example of 12/11:

- **Level 1 -- The unit fraction (e.g., 1/11).** The basic iterable unit. A student
  reasoning at one level treats the whole *as* the unit fraction -- they can partition
  but cannot yet relate the part back to the whole as a composite.

- **Level 2 -- The composite unit (e.g., 12/11).** The result of iterating the unit
  fraction twelve times. A student reasoning at two levels understands the whole as a
  composite unit *of* unit fractions. They can establish the multiplicative relation:
  five 1/5-ounce bags make one ounce (Olive & Vomvoridi, 2006).

- **Level 3 -- The referent whole (e.g., 11/11).** The composite unit that defines the
  *size* of the unit fraction. A student reasoning at three levels can simultaneously
  coordinate all three: the unit fraction, the composite fraction, and the referent
  whole. This is required for improper fractions and for fraction multiplication
  ("fractions of fractions").

### Why Three Levels Matter

Hackenberg's key finding: **the construction of improper fractions requires having
interiorized three levels of units.** Students who have interiorized only two levels
may operate with fractions greater than one, but they do not produce improper fractions
in the full sense. This called for a revision of Steffe's hypothesis that the splitting
operation alone was sufficient -- Hackenberg showed that splitting is necessary but not
sufficient without three-level interiorization.

The practical consequence is visible in the difficulty of fraction multiplication.
Taking 1/3 of 2/5 of a candy bar requires reasoning across all three levels
simultaneously: the unit fraction (1/15), the composite result (2/15), and the
referent whole (the original bar). Olive and Vomvoridi (2006) document a student, Tim,
who could recursively partition the bar and count the resulting pieces (15) but could
not easily *name* the result as a fraction of the original whole -- precisely because
naming requires a comparison among three levels of units.

### Connection to Whole-Number Multiplicative Concepts

Boyce and Norton (2016) interviewed 50 sixth-graders and found **commonality in the
number of levels of units-within-units structures that students construct for whole
numbers and fractions** -- consistent with Steffe's reorganization hypothesis. Their
results suggest that "fractions may be a fertile domain for elementary and middle
grades students to continue to develop whole number understandings." The relationship
is bidirectional: fraction work can strengthen whole-number multiplicative concepts,
not only the reverse.

Ulrich (2015) synthesizes decades of research into a four-stage framework of how
students develop their ability to construct and coordinate arithmetical units. She
notes that "seemingly unrelated difficulties dealing with fractions, negative numbers,
algebraic notation, and combinatorial reasoning can all be related back to the
students' counting schemes with whole numbers."

Viegut et al. (2024) found that units coordination and fraction schemes were better
predictors of eighth graders' algebra knowledge than fraction magnitude or fraction
arithmetic skills, confirming the central role of units coordination structures across
mathematical domains.

## The Continuity Question

**Can fractions emerge from whole-number crisis rather than requiring a categorical
leap?**

The research literature supports a **continuity position** -- but a nuanced one. The
transition is not seamless assimilation; it requires genuine reorganization. The
evidence from three converging lines of research:

### 1. The Reorganization Hypothesis (Steffe/Olive)

Steffe and Olive's longitudinal teaching experiments show children constructing
fraction schemes *from* their counting-based number sequences. The ENS gets
reorganized into a connected number sequence (CNS) when applied to continuous
quantities. The case studies of Jason, Laura, and Joe document this reorganization
in detail, including its failures and partial successes.

Boyce and Norton (2016) provide quantitative confirmation: the number of levels of
units coordination students achieve in whole-number contexts predicts their fraction
scheme construction, consistent with reorganization rather than categorical
discontinuity.

### 2. Conceptual Change Theory (Vosniadou and colleagues)

The framework theory approach to conceptual change describes fraction learning as a
"slow, gradual process" constrained by the presupposition of discreteness that children
build from their whole-number experience (Vamvakoussi & Vosniadou, 2004). Children do
not make an instantaneous leap; they generate **synthetic concepts** -- intermediate
states that bridge their whole-number perspective with the rational-number perspective.

These synthetic concepts include the whole-number misconception (0.25 > 0.7 because
25 > 7) and the fraction misconception (longer decimals are smaller because smaller
parts). Durkin and Rittle-Johnson (2014) document how these misconceptions form,
persist, and gradually resolve through conceptual change.

The key insight: misconceptions are not failures of learning but evidence that students
are actively trying to assimilate new information into existing conceptual structures.
They are the visible traces of the reorganization process.

### 3. Integrated Magnitude Theory (Siegler)

Siegler et al. (2011) propose an integrated theory of whole-number and fraction
development, arguing that the centrality of *magnitude knowledge* is a commonality
across both domains. Accuracy of fraction magnitude representations is closely related
to fraction arithmetic proficiency and overall mathematics achievement. This suggests a
shared cognitive substrate rather than separate systems.

Lin et al. (2021), in a meta-analytic structural equation modeling study of 6,096
students, found that conceptual whole-number knowledge is a "significant and stable
predictor of conceptual and procedural fraction knowledge." However, students
experiencing mathematics difficulty showed a distinct pattern where whole-number
knowledge had *lesser* impact on conceptual fraction knowledge -- suggesting that the
reorganization pathway may be harder to traverse for some learners.

### The Verdict: Continuity Through Crisis

The convergent finding is that fractions do not require a categorical leap, but the
continuity is not trivial. The transition requires:

- **Reorganization** of counting-based schemes to incorporate continuous quantity
  (Steffe/Olive)
- **A qualitative shift** from two-level to three-level unit coordination
  (Hackenberg)
- **Gradual conceptual change** through synthetic intermediate concepts
  (Vosniadou)

In the UMEDCTA framework, this maps directly to the ORR cycle: the learner's existing
whole-number strategies produce a *crisis* when applied to fractional quantities. The
crisis is not "I need a completely different kind of number" but rather "my counting
scheme cannot partition the unit, and I need to reorganize it so it can." The
reorganization preserves the iterative core (counting) while transforming the strategic
shell (how units are coordinated).

## What "Tuning the PDA to 7" Could Mean

The UMEDCTA manuscript claims that fractions are "fractal expansion of the base
dialectic" -- that rational numbers are not a separate ontological category but the
continuation of the same dialectic past the unit boundary. Whole numbers emerge through
iterative compression (multiplying by the base); rational numbers emerge through
iterative decompression (dividing by the base).

This claim gives a specific meaning to "tuning the PDA to 7":

### The Base-7 Counting Scheme

In the existing system, the counting PDA (`counting2.pl`) implements base-10 counting
with carry states. When nine ones become one ten, this is sublation -- negation,
preservation, elevation. The automaton's stack encodes the compressed history of
counting.

A counting scheme "tuned to 7" would be a PDA that carries at 7 instead of 10. In
such a system:

- Seven itself is written as (10)_7 -- the first step of the outward dialectic
- 1/7 becomes (0.1)_7 -- the fundamental unit fraction of that base system, the first
  step of the *inward* dialectic
- The structural relationship between place values repeats at finer resolutions:
  (Hundreds : Tens) :: (Tens : Ones) :: (Ones : Sevenths) :: (Sevenths : Forty-ninths)

### What Steffe's Framework Says About This

In Steffe's terms, "tuning the PDA to 7" corresponds to the child constructing a new
**partitioning scheme with 7 as the partitioning unit**. The equi-partitioning scheme
that creates sevenths is structurally analogous to the grouping scheme that creates
tens -- both involve taking a collection and imposing a uniform structure on it.

The difference, and this is the source of difficulty, is that the base-10 system is
*normatively entrenched* -- it is the single standard that the child's entire counting
experience has reinforced. A base-7 partitioning scheme has no such normative backing.
The child must construct it locally, for the specific purpose of understanding sevenths,
and then hold it in coordination with every other partitioning scheme they might need.

This is why Hackenberg's three-level coordination matters here. In base 10, the child
needs only two levels: ones and tens (and the relationship between them). In fraction
arithmetic with variable denominators, the child needs three levels: the unit fraction
(one seventh), the composite fraction (three sevenths), and the referent whole -- and
the referent whole is *not* normatively fixed the way "ten" is in base-10 arithmetic.

### Incommensurable Norms

The manuscript frames fraction addition (e.g., 1/7 + 1/5) as a confrontation between
**incommensurable normative structures**. The fraction 1/7 means "one unit derived from
a system where seven such units compose the whole." The fraction 1/5 means "one unit
derived from a system where five such units compose the whole." The embodied actions of
partitioning-by-seven and partitioning-by-five are materially different, incompatible
operations.

Finding the common denominator is reinterpreted as **dialectical synthesis through
recursive partitioning**: partition the 1/5 unit according to the seven-fold norm, and
partition the 1/7 unit according to the five-fold norm. The resulting unit (1/35) is
the synthesized unit of measure -- the finest resolution where both schemas align.

This is sublation: the sufficiency of the original denominators is *negated*, their
partitioning capacities are *preserved* within the structure of 35, and the system
*elevates* to finer fractal resolution.

### What This Means for the System

For the Prolog formalization, "tuning the PDA to 7" would mean generating a new
counting automaton parameterized by the denominator. Each such automaton is a
recollection -- a compressed temporal experience of "what it means to count in groups
of 7." Under this reading:

- 1/7 is the first tick of the base-7 automaton
- 3/7 is three ticks of the base-7 automaton
- The automaton itself IS the denominator, understood as a recollected process
- Common denominators require synthesizing a meta-automaton that subsumes both base-7
  and base-5 counting -- the LCM as dialectical synthesis

The existing `fsm_synthesis_engine.pl` cannot do this because its `make_base` strategy
hardcodes base 10. The extension would require a parameterized `make_base(N)` primitive
and a second-order synthesis operation that finds the LCM base.

Whether this is tractable as an FSM synthesis problem or whether it requires something
genuinely new (recursive strategy composition, not flat FSM generation) is the open
question. The FRACTION_CRISIS_ASSESSMENT.md identifies this as Gap 3.
