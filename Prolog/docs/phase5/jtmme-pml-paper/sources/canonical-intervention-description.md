# The Implicit Web: AI-Scaffolded Dyadic Discussion in a Pluralistic Education Course

*Theodore M. Savich, March 2026*

---

## The Problem

I teach M300, "Teaching in a Pluralistic Society," a social studies methods course for preservice teachers at Indiana University. Indiana is a conservative state. The materials I inherited carry what might be interpreted as a progressive slant. A lecturer in IU's School of Social Work was suspended for six weeks after a student reported her for teaching material that overlapped substantially with my own reading list. When she returned, the university placed monitors in her classroom. 

I cannot teach as though endorsing any ideology, nor would I want to. The goal is to teach preservice teachers how to facilitate difficult conversations while being genuinely open to perspectives from any part of the political spectrum. This constraint turned out to be productive. If I could not tell students what to think, I had to create conditions where they could think together.

The deeper problem is connecting. My small groups work well — students talk to each other readily in pairs and triads. Whole-class discussion with me at the front fails. Students don't address one another; they address me, and I end up performing the thinking I want them to do. I have twenty-two students. The number of possible pairwise connections between them is 231. I cannot hold that many relationships in working memory. I cannot track whose experience of alienation in a gym maps onto whose experience of alienation in a Buddhist meditation center, or how a student's transition from moral relativism to universalism after watching *Santosh* might illuminate another student's experience of hypocrisy at a church service. The connections are there. I can sense them as I read student work. I cannot articulate them all, and any attempt to do so in lecture would contradict the pedagogical principle at stake: that understanding emerges through participation, not transmission.

This problem is not specific to M300. It is the problem Smith and Stein's Five Practices for Orchestrating Productive Mathematics Discussions name as *connecting* — the fifth and hardest practice, the one teachers consistently struggle with. In my E343 math methods course, I teach the Five Practices as a sequence: anticipate, monitor, select, sequence, connect. The first four practices are cognitively demanding but tractable. Connecting — the act of drawing out relationships between different students' contributions so the class can construct shared understanding — requires the instructor to hold a network of ideas in mind simultaneously, in real time, while also managing the social dynamics of a room full of people. In mathematics, the connections are between solution strategies.  In M300, the connections are between lived experiences — which carry existential and identity weight that mathematical strategies do not. But the cognitive demand on the instructor is structurally similar: hold a network of contributions in mind, identify productive relationships, and render them in a form students can take up.

## What I Did

Students wrote "Experience of Difference" papers. The assignment asked them to place themselves in an unfamiliar environment — attend a religious service not their own, live on food assistance for three days, spend a day without technology, enter a gendered space they normally avoid — and write reflectively about it. They wrote well, for the most part. Twenty-two students produced 123 typed pages of personal, often vulnerable text. I spent days reading and commenting on them. I am a slow reader (trained as a math major, not a humanist) and just reading the papers carefully and commenting is where I would usually stop. Sometimes just reading is where I stop, which is terrible, because students need intersubjective recognition to develop. But comments on papers land, so often, with a thud. The null response.

My feedback accumulated in a single text file. As I wrote, I kept sensing connections between students I could not articulate. Two students seemed to be experiencing what Brandom would call asymmetric distributions of authority and responsibility — one from a position of excess responsibility (the instinct to protect others from discomfort), the other from a position of excess authority (the assumption that proximity to diversity constitutes understanding). Another student had transitioned from moral relativism to universalism in a way that would illuminate a classmate's experience of hypocrisy. The connections were inferential — they lived in the space between what students wrote and what their writing implied — and they exceeded what I could hold in my head or render in marginal comments.

I replaced student names in my feedback with coded identifiers. I then fed my own feedback — not student work — to an AI system (Gemini, using NotebookLM populated with my primary theoretical sources: Hegel, Brandom, Habermas, Carspecken and some course readings). I asked the AI to thematize my feedback in relation to those sources, identifying core paradoxes and conundrums. The AI produced a lengthy report organized around themes: technology as existential buffer, the mental load of poverty, navigating gendered spaces, the mechanics of moral judgment, faith and implicit connection, ideology and the relationship between discomfort and growth.

The themes were scaffolding. But themes are not connections. Themes group; connections link.

I then iterated — through several rounds of prompting and substantial manual rewriting — toward dyadic questions: prompts designed for specific pairs of students, crafted to make implicit connections between their experiences explicit. The AI generated candidates; I rebuilt them. Calibrating tone, removing inappropriate inferences, verifying I had not misrepresented anyone's experience, deciding which connections were pedagogically productive versus merely clever versus slop. The AI made it feasible to consider a large space of possible connections. The pedagogical judgment about which connections to make explicit, and how to frame them as questions rather than assertions, remained mine. For the few students who hadn't submitted papers, I connected them to themes about inhibition and suppression and asked them to bring a fresh perspective.

Each question named what each student had written (in my paraphrase), identified the conceptual tension between them, and invited them to work through it together. The questions were not discussion starters in the usual sense. They were hypotheses about inferential relationships between particular people, rendered as invitations to test those hypotheses in conversation.

Throughout this process, I was also building an interactive website for my E343 math methods course on the Five Practices. The website visualizes how assessing and advancing questions orbit around a lesson goal like matter around a gravitational star. As I built the questioning framework for math, I recognized the same structure in what I was doing for M300. The dyadic questions were assessing and advancing questions aimed not at mathematical strategies but at the conceptual connections between students' lived experiences. The "lesson goal," the gravitational star, was not a mathematical target but something I could only gesture at: the idea that we are always already connected at a level deeper than what we can make explicit, and that making those connections explicit through language is both necessary and necessarily incomplete. In Hegel's terms: we are *in*finite beings who must descend to the finite realm to actualize our *in*finite nature.

I created a network diagram — nodes for each student, edges for every possible pairwise connection — and printed it on each student's handout. The diagram was a visual representation of the implicit web: all 231 connections rendered as faint lines, with the student's name at one node. Below the diagram, each student received unique instructions: talk to three or four named classmates, using the dyadic questions I had written for those specific pairs. A second round offered open-ended questions about the architecture of belief, the friction of growth, the limits of language, and systems versus authorship.

The handout opened: "Talk to whom you wish, about what you want, in whatever order feels natural." It then specified: "Your goal today is not to debate or find easy agreement. Instead, the aim is to take the dense, vast, and implicit web of connections we call 'being' and translate it into explicit 'knowing.'" It also asked students to assume I had mischaracterized their experiences and to correct me — to take responsibility for their own narratives.

Before distributing the handouts, I displayed the Five Practices website on the projector. I wanted students to understand the pedagogical structure they were enacting. The questions were not arbitrary; they emerged from a systematic process of monitoring, selecting, sequencing, and connecting — the same process I was teaching them to use in math instruction. The implicit web diagram was itself a representation of what connecting looks like before it happens: all potential, no selection, no sequence.

Then I handed out the papers and walked to the back of the room.

## What Students Did

For seventy-five minutes, students moved around the room talking to each other. I listened. Occasionally I separated pairs who had been talking too long, redirecting them to new partners. The questions did the work.

The form of the intervention matters here. A question does not assert a connection — it proposes one. It leaves room for the respondent to reject the premise, redirect the conversation, or discover something the question did not anticipate. This is what I mean by letting the implicit remain implicit. I did not tell students that their experiences were connected. I asked whether they might be, and in asking, I gave them the authority to determine the answer. The question form is consistent with what I take to be the central pedagogical insight of my career: explication is necessary, but understanding does not terminate in explication. We move from implicit being to explicit knowing and back to implicit being — enriched, but never fully articulated. What the Five Practices call "connecting" is this movement: making relationships between ideas sayable without pretending that saying exhausts them.

At the end of class, I wrote on the board: "Hegel said something like: 'We are *in*finite (non-finite; subjects; not-objects) beings who must descend to the finite realm in order to actualize our *in*finite nature.'"

That idea, the heart of my philosophy of education, has been extraordinarily difficult to express in writing and with graduate students that I had never even attempted to articulate it with undergraduates. Telling students about the *in*finite contradicts the term. The *in*finite, by definition, resists finalization. But after seventy-five minutes of making implicit connections explicit through conversation, the Hegel quote did not land with the null response I have come to expect. A student stayed after class and asked if I had ever seen *The Perks of Being a Wallflower*. A line in the film is apparently "we are *in*finite." She recommended it to me. In pragmatist terms, understanding is the capacity to respond in normatively appropriate ways. She responded. A movie recommendation is not strong evidence of philosophical comprehension, but it is sufficient to demonstrate that she grasped — at least partially, which is all anyone ever gets — what I was trying to say.

## Why It Matters

Three things converge in this intervention that I have not encountered together in the literature on productive classroom discussion.

First, the cognitive overload problem. The Five Practices literature acknowledges that connecting is the hardest practice. What it does not address is what happens when the number of student contributions exceeds the instructor's working memory. Twenty-two students producing unique reflections on lived experience generate a combinatorial space of potential connections that no instructor can navigate unaided. AI functioned here as a cognitive tool for the instructor, not as a replacement for pedagogical judgment but as a way to surface candidate connections that I could then evaluate, edit, and render as questions. The AI did not interact with students. It interacted with my interpretive labor.

Second, the form of questioning. Dyadic questions addressed to specific pairs of students are not standard discussion prompts. They are relational scaffolds: each question encodes a hypothesis about the inferential relationship between two people's experiences and invites those people to test the hypothesis. This is different from asking a group to "discuss Theme X." It is closer to what happens in a good mathematical discussion when the teacher says, "Student A used a table and Student B used a graph — can you two figure out how these strategies are related?" Except here, the "strategies" are ways of being in the world, and the "mathematical goal" is the recognition that one's own experience is intelligible in relation to someone else's.

Third, the relationship between implicitness and explicitness. Much of what makes discussion productive is precisely what resists full articulation. The web of connections between students is always already there — in the shared fact of being embodied, social, and finite beings trying to make sense of difference. The intervention does not claim to make this web fully explicit. It makes parts of it sayable and trusts that the unsayable remainder does its own work. The student who recommended *Perks of Being a Wallflower* demonstrated this: she did not paraphrase Hegel. She responded from within her own horizon of understanding, and that response was enough for the context - teaching in a pluralistic society.

## The Role of AI

AI served as a tool for managing the cognitive demand of connecting — the fifth practice — at a scale that exceeds unaided human capacity. Specifically:

1. **Thematizing instructor feedback.** I fed my own written feedback on student papers (with student names replaced by coded identifiers) to an AI system equipped with my theoretical source library. The AI identified recurring themes, paradoxes, and tensions across my feedback. (University policy prohibits using AI to evaluate student work. I thematized my own interpretive labor, not students' papers.)

2. **Generating candidate connections.** From the thematic analysis, I iteratively prompted the AI to produce dyadic questions linking specific pairs of students. These candidates required substantial manual editing — calibrating tone, removing inappropriate inferences, ensuring I had not misrepresented a student's experience, and deciding which connections were pedagogically productive versus merely clever.

3. **Handling combinatorial complexity.** With 22 students and 231 possible pairwise connections, the question was never "should I connect these students?" but "which of these many possible connections are worth making explicit today?" AI made it feasible to consider a much larger space of possibilities than I could survey on my own. I then selected, sequenced, and edited — the same practices I teach in E343.

The AI did not see student work. It did not interact with students. It processed my thinking about student work, organized through my theoretical commitments, and returned candidate structures that I then rebuilt by hand.

## What Comes Next

This intervention happened once, in one class, with no systematic data collection beyond my own observations and student comments afterward. It is a proof of concept, not a study, not yet a design. The question is whether it can be formalized, iterated, and studied: whether the design can be refined through cycles of enactment and analysis, and whether its effects on discussion quality, student understanding, and instructor cognition can be characterized rigorously. What I have is a novel use case for AI, augmenting the instructor's capacity to connect student thinking at a scale that exceeds unaided cognition, and a single enactment that suggests the approach is worth pursuing.

The intervention sits at the intersection of several active research areas: productive classroom discussion, AI-augmented pedagogy, collaborative learning, scaffolding, and the role of implicitness in understanding. It needs a research home and collaborators who know how to build one.
