# §2 Best Day — praxis that preceded the formalization

*Target: 900–1,100 words. Based on `sources/canonical-intervention-description.md`; follow that source tightly and do not elaborate beyond it.*

---

M300 is a methods course called "Teaching in a Pluralistic Society," required of preservice teachers at Indiana University. Indiana is a conservative state. The materials I inherited carried what could be interpreted as a progressive slant. A lecturer in the School of Social Work, teaching a reading list that overlapped substantially with mine, was suspended for six weeks after a student report and returned to find monitors placed in her classroom. The political constraint was real.

I could not teach as though endorsing any ideology, nor would I want to. The aim was to teach preservice teachers how to facilitate difficult conversations while remaining genuinely open to perspectives from across the political spectrum. The constraint turned out to be productive. If I could not tell students what to think, I had to build conditions under which they could think together.

The deeper problem was *connecting*. Smith and Stein's *Five Practices for Orchestrating Productive Mathematics Discussions* names connecting — drawing out the relationships between different students' contributions so the class can build shared understanding — as the fifth and hardest practice. Teachers consistently struggle with it. In mathematics, the connections are between solution strategies. In M300, the connections are between lived experiences, which carry existential and identity weight that mathematical strategies do not. But the cognitive demand on the instructor is structurally similar: hold a network of contributions in mind, identify productive relationships, and render them in a form that students can take up.

The numbers make connecting unaided impossible. Twenty-two students means 231 possible pairwise connections. Even restricting attention to the connections that matter pedagogically, the space exceeds what I can hold in working memory while also managing the social dynamics of a room.

## What I did

Students wrote "Experience of Difference" papers. The assignment asked each to place themselves in an unfamiliar environment — a religious service not their own, three days on food assistance, a day without technology, a gendered space they normally avoid — and write reflectively about the experience. Twenty-two students produced roughly 123 pages of often vulnerable text. I read them carefully and commented on each. My feedback, collected in a single file with student names replaced by coded identifiers, accumulated into something I could read back.

As I read my own feedback, I could sense connections between students that I could not articulate. Two students appeared to be working through what Brandom would call asymmetric distributions of authority and responsibility — one from excess responsibility, the other from excess authority. Another had moved from moral relativism to universalism in a way that could illuminate a classmate's experience of hypocrisy. The connections were inferential. They lived in the space between what students wrote and what the writing implied, and they exceeded what I could track unaided.

I fed my feedback — not student work; university policy prohibits using AI to evaluate student work — to a NotebookLM populated with Hegel, Brandom, Habermas, Carspecken, and course readings. The AI thematized my interpretive labor. Themes, however, are not connections. Themes group; connections link.

I iterated toward dyadic questions: prompts addressed to specific pairs of students, each encoding a hypothesis about a connection between two people's experiences and inviting them to test the hypothesis together. The AI generated candidates. I rebuilt them by hand — calibrating tone, removing inappropriate inferences, verifying I had not misrepresented anyone's experience, deciding which connections were pedagogically productive and which were merely clever or slop. The AI made a larger space of candidate connections available than I could have surveyed alone. The judgment about which connections to make explicit remained mine.

For a given class session, I printed a network diagram showing all 231 potential pairwise connections as faint lines, with each student's name at their node. Below the diagram, each student received a unique set of instructions: talk to three or four named classmates, using the dyadic questions I had written for those specific pairs. A second round offered open-ended questions. The handout opened: "Talk to whom you wish, about what you want, in whatever order feels natural." It then asked students to assume I had mischaracterized their experiences and to correct me if so.

Before distributing the handouts, I displayed a Five Practices website I had been building for my math methods course. The website visualizes how assessing and advancing questions orbit a lesson goal like matter around a gravitational star. I wanted students to understand the pedagogical structure they were about to enact. The dyadic questions were not arbitrary; they emerged from the same monitor-select-sequence-connect practice I teach in math instruction. Then I handed out the packets and walked to the back of the room.

## What the intervention is not

It is not a study. It was one class, one session, no systematic data collection beyond my own observations and student comments afterward. It is not the ground for empirical claims. It is the praxis that forced the question the formalization is trying to answer: what vocabulary is adequate for tracking the pragmatic stances students take up when invited to recognize connections between their own experiences and someone else's?

Why it worked is not fully reducible to the intervention mechanics. I spent the whole semester building a classroom where students could say "no" to a question. Discussion handouts voiced critiques of their own prompts. A guided meditation with toes and sublation. A song from my dissertation that modeled vulnerability. The question form gave students authority to reject the premise. The existential fear of being misrecognized was treated with care throughout. The intervention drew on those conditions; it did not create them from scratch.

The AI's role was cognitive tooling on my own interpretive labor. AI did not interact with students. AI did not see student work. AI processed my feedback on student work, organized by my theoretical commitments, and returned candidate structures that I then rebuilt by hand. The AI-in-the-classroom story is not this story. This story is about what becomes possible when an instructor can consider a larger combinatorial space of connections than unaided cognition can survey.

Best Day worked. I have been trying to formalize the topology of intersubjective space since 2017. PML is what the formalization currently looks like. The next sections describe what PML provides, what phenomenological ground it leans on, what its axioms for the subjective mode of validity are, and what it cannot yet tell.
