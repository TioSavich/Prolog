# §7 Closing — what the author would like feedback on

*Target: 400–500 words. No new claims. The feedback list, explicit, followed by a one-paragraph closing that names the genre.*

---

This paper is a report, submitted to a venue whose editorial stance welcomes work in progress. The author is asking the reviewer for feedback on five specific questions.

**Is the Carspecken reading of the feeling body defensible?** §3 leans on Carspecken's horizon analysis to ground subjective validity in pre-reflective embodied experience rather than in introspection-as-observation. The draft of §3 in the project repository marks several passages as trawl-gaps — places where Carspecken citations need sourcing from the author's notebook archive. A reviewer familiar with Carspecken's 1999 *Four Scenes* and the *Limits of Knowledge* material would know whether the author's account of the feeling body holds together, or whether the account has drifted from Carspecken's own framework in ways the author has not noticed.

**Do the S-mode axioms look right to readers more at home in modal logic than the author?** §4 presents seven material inferences as plain-English dialectical movements. The author is not a modal logician. The axioms are drawn from Hegel and Brandom and encoded in a Prolog sequent calculus that passes internal-consistency tests. Whether the axioms are the right ones — whether, for instance, the fork at awareness (possibility of release vs. possibility of fixation) should be represented as two licenses or as one license with a disjunctive consequent — is a question the author cannot answer from inside the framework.

**Is the neurosymbolic test proposal plausible, or does it break for reasons the author has not foreseen?** §5 sketches a test that couples an LLM's operator-profile perception with the Prolog's inferential engine, tested against the TalkMoves corpus. The author is specifically interested in failure modes that would not be obvious from inside the proposal. Classroom talk has structural features that may not align with the dialectical rhythm the axioms claim; the author's guess is that failures would be diagnostic rather than fatal, but the guess is a guess.

**Is the Best Day intervention interesting for this community, and if so, where is its research home?** §2 presents the intervention as praxis. The author has not done the research design that would turn the praxis into a studied practice. Readers with experience in design-based research, in AI-augmented pedagogy, or in the Five Practices literature may know where such work finds its home.

**What prior work is the author missing?** Every framework-proposing paper is in danger of reinventing. The author has the Brandom, Carspecken, Hegel, and Habermas reading well in hand and has worked with the recent discourse-analysis literature up to the TalkMoves line. Gaps likely exist in other traditions — in category-theoretic approaches to modality, in the more formal wings of the recognitive tradition, in classroom discourse work outside the North American corpus. Pointers would be welcome.

The paper is an invitation. The work is still moving. The author has given enough structure for a reader to tell him where the structure is wrong.
