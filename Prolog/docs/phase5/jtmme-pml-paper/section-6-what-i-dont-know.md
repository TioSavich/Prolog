# §6 What I don't know

*Target: 700–900 words. First-class section. Not humility-performance — specific, sourced limits.*

---

The paper's claim is that PML's S-mode axioms form a consistent vocabulary for one dimension of classroom discourse. The claim's limits are themselves a substantive part of what the paper reports. A recent audit of the code against its documentation produced a set of fact-sheets at `docs/phase5/fact-sheet-<module>.md` that are the basis for the limits listed here. Each limit below references a specific file, section, or audit finding a reader can check.

**The objective and normative modes are not on the same footing as the subjective mode.** PML's `o` and `n` wrappers are permissive — they accept any proposition as a term. The code has four inter-modal transfer rules (the "Oobleck" family in `pml/semantic_axioms.pl`) that move claims between modes, plus one hylomorphic shift (`[o(P)] → n(P)`, "objective proof licenses normative commitment") that landed during the audit this paper is built on. The reverse direction — normative commitment licensing subjective embrace, which Carspecken's horizon analysis would call *internalization* — is named in the surrounding prose and not coded. The reason is honest: the conditions under which N becomes S depend on trace-grounding via the I-feeling mechanism, and the conditions are not yet specifiable as a rule. The S-mode is the developed fragment; the O and N modes are scaffolding and one rule. The fact-sheet for `pml/` records this imbalance; §4 of this paper presents only S-mode for the same reason.

**Inter-modal transitions may be scene-dependent rather than universal.** The Oobleck principle — subjective compressive necessity transfers to objective compressive necessity under certain pressures, like oobleck under fast impact becoming solid — reads well in the scenes where the author first articulated it and reads unevenly in scenes where the pressures are different. The four Oobleck rules in the Prolog fire unconditionally on any matching formula; whether that universality is a fact about the grammar of recognition or a fact about the scenes the rules were extracted from is not demonstrated in code. The framework is called a logic, but it behaves, at the inter-modal boundary, more like musical notation: a grammar for a particular family of movements, not yet shown to cover the whole space.

**The torus topology of intersubjective space is a heuristic, not a proved structure.** The author has been developing a geometric picture of the space of reasons — a torus, specifically — since 2017. The picture is suggestive and has guided the axiom choices. It is not formally established by the Prolog. A torus is a specific topological object with specific properties; nothing in the PML code proves or uses those properties. The heuristic may be the right one and may eventually be formalized. The current state is: the torus is a working metaphor the author has found productive; the code does not yet earn it.

**The phenomenological grounding is uncertain.** The author can describe the feeling body from within Carspecken's account. The author cannot guarantee that the formal operators track the phenomenological structure they are meant to track. The `arche_trace` attribute, presented in §3 as the formal analog of the feeling body's refusal of objectification, is a mechanical move that is philosophically motivated but not philosophically demonstrated. A reader standing at the hermeneutic circle objection — that the formalism was chosen to match a reading of the phenomenology, so of course it matches — would not be wrong to raise it. The paper's defense is that the mechanical refusal of unification is specific enough to produce a distinctive proof object (`erasure(...)` rather than `proof(...)`) and that this distinctiveness is testable. The defense is that the limit is inspectable, not that the limit is absent.

**The cost accounting in the code is approximate.** A recent fix made direct callers of the `incur_cost/1` predicate accumulate into a module-local counter, and the meta-interpreter-routed path continues to track through its own inference budget. The two counters are not reconciled; a single run through the system produces two numbers rather than one. For the purposes of this paper — which does not lean on cost as a load-bearing claim — the approximation is acceptable. For a future paper that wanted to compare cognitive-load predictions across strategies, the approximation is a limit. The `docs/phase5/fact-sheet-formalization.md` and `fact-sheet-strategies.md` both record this precisely.

**The system's "learning loop" does not actually close in code.** The learner module in `learner/` has a function called `reorganize_system/2` that refers to a predicate `discover_strategy/3` which is not defined in the stripped code. A separate synthesis engine is a shim that always fails. The ORR cycle (Observe-React-Reorganize) that the system's documentation describes is complete at the Observe and React stages; the Reorganize stage is a gap. This is relevant to §4 only indirectly — the S-mode axioms do not depend on the learner's reorganization — but a reader working through the full project should know the loop is not closed. The `docs/phase5/fact-sheet-learner.md` has the specifics.

**The framework is complicated enough to illuminate anything.** This is the meta-limit. A twelve-operator modal logic crossed with a trace mechanism crossed with a sequent calculus has plenty of apparatus for explaining whatever the author points it at. *Illumination is therefore cheap*. The objective validity of the framework — whether the apparatus is tracking something real about classroom discourse, or whether the apparatus is an elaborate way of reconstructing what the author already believed — is the open question. The neurosymbolic test sketch in §5 is the author's best current proposal for turning the question into something a reviewer could settle.

Each limit above is a specific claim about a specific place in the codebase. They are not collectively a retraction. They are the author's best attempt at showing the reviewer where to press hardest.
