# Best Day Grant Proposal — Honest Assessment

**Module drawn on:** `pml/` (primary), with reference to `learner/` and
`arche-trace/`
**Target venues:** NSF DRK-12, NSF CAREER (when eligible), IU internal
seed grant (next cycle)
**Claim the proposal would make:** The Best Day intervention uses PML as a
measurement instrument to analyze instructor feedback on student writing,
identify pairs of students whose epistemic positions are complementary,
and generate dyadic discussion prompts that create conditions for
intersubjective recognition. AI is used to design structured encounters
at scale; students interact with each other, not with AI. The proposal
frames this as a measurement-and-intervention study grounded in catastrophe
theory (for detecting transitions) and inferentialism (for justifying the
measurement).

---

## What the project already provides for a grant

PML has internal consistency. 28/28 core tests pass. The 12-operator
framework is structurally coherent enough to defend in a Significance
section as a measurement instrument *in principle*, even if feasibility
has not been empirically demonstrated.

The intervention design is articulated. The project vision memory
(`.auto-memory/project_vision.md`) is clear: AI analyzes instructor
feedback; students are paired by complementary PML profiles; AI generates
the dyadic prompts but never interacts with students. This is the core
procedural story a grant reviewer needs.

The seed grant has already been submitted. `.auto-memory/seed_grant_status.md`
confirms the IU seed-grant cycle has closed; the grant was submitted before
April 13, 2026. The next cycle will come around. Between now and then is
time to strengthen what the next submission can rest on.

A measurement rationale is available. `docs/LITERATURE_BRIDGE.md` and
`docs/LITERATURE_SEARCH_APRIL2026.md` catalogue the relevant literature:
Hennessy 2020 (coding-scheme methodology), Ng 2021 (instrument
development), van der Maas & Molenaar 1992 (catastrophe theory as a
methodology for stage transitions), Hackenberg 2025 (decentering and
responsive teaching), the 2017 MERJ inferentialism special issue.

Faculty allies are mapped. `.auto-memory/faculty_allies.md` names four
tenured IU faculty (Hackenberg, Jacobson, Tillema, Weltsek) and the entry
point for each. Erik Jacobson's entry point — PML as a testable measurement
instrument — is precisely the claim the grant rests on.

## What a competitive grant proposal needs that does not yet exist

**Inter-rater pilot data.** This is the same gap the feasibility paper
faces, and the same fix serves both. Reviewers will want Cohen's kappa
for at least the person-position and polarity channels on a pilot
transcript. Without this, the measurement claim is proposed, not
demonstrated. One pilot serves two products — the paper and the grant.

**Preliminary data on pair complementarity.** The intervention assumes
that two students with different PML profiles will generate more
productive discussion than two with similar profiles. This is testable
— with a small set of transcripts and an AI-generated pairing, plus a
manual pairing, a reviewer can see what "complementary" means concretely.
No such pilot has been run.

**Catastrophe-theoretic operationalization.** The grant claims the
measurement tracks developmental transitions, not just stable states.
The catastrophe-theory literature (van der Maas & Molenaar 1992,
Stamovlasis 2016) specifies five criteria — bimodality, hysteresis,
sudden jumps, inaccessibility, divergence — that a measurement must
exhibit to qualify as catastrophe-theoretic. The proposal has to show
how PML data *could* exhibit these, even hypothetically. This is a
theoretical section; it can be written before any empirical work.

**The intervention protocol.** What does the Best Day intervention look
like procedurally, week by week, in an E343 or similar methods course?
How does the AI-generated pairing happen? What does the instructor do
with the prompt? How is student consent handled? What is the data
collection cycle? A grant reviewer reads this as the Research Design
section; it has to be fully specified.

**Human-subjects approval strategy.** An intervention involving IU
students needs IRB. The `legal:` skills in the plugin environment
reference FERPA, IRB, and AI-use compliance for IU. The proposal has to
lay out the compliance path — which depends on whether AI is analyzing
student-generated content (stricter) or only instructor feedback (looser).
The vision memory is clear: AI never interacts with students; students
interact with each other. That framing is both pedagogically and
regulatorily favorable. The proposal has to make this visible.

**A theory of change for the intervention.** Why would dyadic pairing by
complementary PML profiles produce better outcomes than random pairing?
The theoretical argument is: two students in different modal modes have
discursive work to do to establish shared ground; that work is where
learning happens. This is a Carspecken/Habermas claim about
intersubjective recognition. It needs to be stated as a theory of change
— with a diagram, ideally — so a DRK-12 reviewer can see the mechanism.

**Faculty co-investigator commitments.** A competitive grant needs named
co-investigators. The faculty allies memory identifies four candidates.
None is currently a committed co-PI. Converting "intellectual endorsement"
(the faculty allies' current role) into "named co-I" takes conversation
and institutional paperwork. This is people work, not paper work.

## Where the claim would exceed what is formalized

Three places the grant would need to walk carefully.

First: PML as measurement vs. PML as hypothesis. The proposal will want
to say PML *measures* decentering, compressive/expansive polarity, and
S/O/N mode distribution. What the code supports is that PML is internally
consistent and expresses these distinctions formally. Whether the
distinctions are reliably observable in real classroom discourse is
exactly the empirical question the grant would fund. The proposal's
Specific Aims section has to frame PML as "the measurement instrument
we propose to validate" rather than "the measurement instrument we have."

Second: catastrophe theory as methodology vs. catastrophe theory as
metaphor. van der Maas & Molenaar's five criteria are sharp. The
learner module's tension dynamics exhibit some of them (the
second-derivative stability check approximates sudden jumps) but not
all. The proposal must be clear: we are using catastrophe theory as a
*methodology* for how to detect transitions in PML data — not claiming
that the catastrophe surface itself has been empirically traced in
student discourse.

Third: Best Day as intervention vs. Best Day as proof-of-concept. A
DRK-12 grant wants effect sizes, power calculations, randomized
assignment. The first cycle of Best Day is likely a feasibility study
(small n, mixed methods, design-research orientation). The proposal
needs to say so explicitly and point toward the larger study as a
later-cycle or CAREER-level ambition.

## The single largest risk

The grant's most vulnerable claim is also its strongest claim: that AI
can be used to create conditions for human-human recognition without
itself occupying the pedagogical relationship. This is genuinely
innovative; it is also exactly the kind of claim an NSF reviewer will
scrutinize hard. The proposal has to address:

- What exactly does the AI do? (Analyze instructor feedback; identify
  complementary PML profiles; generate dyadic prompts.)
- What does the AI not do? (Interact with students; grade; make
  pedagogical judgments the instructor does not endorse.)
- How is the AI's output audited? (The instructor reviews all prompts
  before distribution; instructor can reject any pairing.)
- What happens if PML misreads a student? (The instructor is the
  appeal layer; the intervention degrades gracefully to instructor
  judgment.)

The vision memory captures the conceptual move: "the contradiction —
using AI to foster human connection — is the point." The grant has to
transform that phrase from a philosophical commitment into a
procedural spec that a reviewer can follow.

## Concrete next moves (if this product is prioritized)

1. **Run the pilot that also serves the feasibility paper.** Same
   transcript, same coders, same Cohen's kappa. Split output: one
   version goes into the paper, one into the grant's preliminary data.
   This is the single highest-leverage piece of work.

2. **Write a one-page theory of change.** Top: the PML measurement
   diagram. Middle: the pairing mechanism. Bottom: the expected
   classroom outcomes. This is what a program officer reads to decide
   whether the grant belongs in DRK-12's portfolio.

3. **Convert one faculty ally into a named co-investigator.** Erik
   Jacobson is the likeliest first conversation — his language is the
   grant's language. A lunch, a one-page project brief shaped to his
   measurement interests, a concrete ask (co-I on the submission).

4. **Draft the Human Subjects protocol.** Because AI does not interact
   with students, the protocol is likely closer to a program-evaluation
   review than a full-board IRB. The `legal:compliance-check` or
   `legal:triage-nda` skills can help here. Start with the AI-use
   disclosure path.

5. **Specify the data-collection cycle.** What is collected (instructor
   feedback; PML codings; student discussion transcripts with consent),
   when (weekly? end-of-unit?), how it is stored, and how it is
   destroyed after analysis. Grant reviewers expect this laid out
   explicitly.

6. **Write the catastrophe-theory section.** Two to three paragraphs
   naming the five criteria and hypothesizing how each could be
   exhibited in PML data. This is where the grant distinguishes itself
   from a standard discourse-analytic study — and where its most
   ambitious theoretical claim lives.

7. **Keep PML's limitations visible.** The proposal should have a
   Limitations and Risk Mitigation section that names the
   phenomenological-grounding concern (from
   `.auto-memory/pml_uncertainty.md`) and explains how the pilot
   addresses it. A reviewer who notices Tio's own uncertainty on the
   page, handled openly, is more likely to fund than one who catches
   the same concern unacknowledged.

## What this product does not force

The grant writes *about* the modules; it does not require them to
change. PML's axioms and operators are the measurement instrument as
currently proposed; the pilot produces the evidence the proposal
cites. The learner module is a technical supporting argument (the
ORR cycle shows the catastrophe surface is computationally modelable)
but the intervention itself operates on transcripts, not on the
learner module. The arche-trace essay's limits-of-formalization
framing is useful context for the grant but not a gating dependency.

Best Day is also the product most sensitive to people work rather than
code work. The bottleneck is not what the modules do; it is what faculty
commitments, IRB approval, and co-PI conversations look like. The code's
role is to provide a defensible measurement instrument that makes the
intervention legible to NSF-style reviewers.

## What the Limitations section of the proposal would say

Honest draft language, for reference:

> The primary limitation of this proposal is that Polarized Modal Logic,
> while internally consistent in its Prolog formalization, has not yet
> been validated as a discourse-analytic measurement instrument.
> Specific Aim 1 addresses this directly: we establish inter-rater
> reliability on a pilot corpus of 10 instructor-feedback documents.
> A secondary limitation is that the Best Day intervention's theory of
> change — that complementary PML profiles produce more productive
> dyadic discussion than random pairing — rests on a philosophical
> commitment to intersubjective recognition (Carspecken, Habermas) that
> has not been operationalized in measurable outcomes. Specific Aim 3
> addresses this: we develop observation instruments for dyadic
> recognition dynamics and test the pairing hypothesis in a small-n
> feasibility study. A third limitation, specific to the AI-mediated
> intervention design, is that any measurement error in the PML coding
> could propagate into pairing decisions. Mitigation: the instructor
> reviews and can override every pairing the system generates; the
> intervention fails gracefully into instructor judgment when PML
> coding confidence is low.

That paragraph, or close to it, is what the grant owes the reviewer —
an honest map of what the intervention rests on and where the risks
live. Nothing in the project's current state makes the paragraph harder
to write; everything in the next-moves list makes it easier.
