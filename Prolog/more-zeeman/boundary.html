Everything so far works for whole numbers. Seven plus five. Thirty-eight plus fifty-five. Ninety-nine plus ninety-nine. The counting PDA handles all of these — expensively, but correctly.

But what about one-third?

The counting PDA counts by ones. Each tally mark is one unit. There is no "one-third of a one." The system doesn't fail — it doesn't crash or give a wrong answer. It has no way to even ask the question. The question is outside its language.

Not a crash. An edge.

The word "not yet" matters. This isn't a wall — it's an edge. The system might grow past it. We just haven't figured out how. And figuring out how requires something the flat counting machine doesn't have: a way to change what counts as "one."

Hackenberg (2010) identifies three levels of unit coordination required for fraction reasoning. The current system handles two levels: ones and composite units (groups of ones, like a ten). Fractions require a third level — a unit-of-units-of-units.

The fraction representation in the Prolog system, fraction/2, is structurally incompatible with recollection/1. A recollection stores a whole-number result. A fraction needs to store a relationship between two quantities — the part and the whole — and that relationship depends on a third quantity: the size of the partitioning unit.

Hackenberg, A. J. (2010). Students' reasoning with reversible multiplicative relationships. Cognition and Instruction, 28(4), 383-432. Also: Hackenberg (2007), JMB, 26(1), 27-47. Three levels of units coordination: (1) the unit fraction (e.g., 1/11), (2) the composite unit (e.g., 12/11 as twelve iterations of the unit fraction), (3) the referent whole (e.g., 11/11 that defines the size of the unit fraction). Hackenberg's key finding: construction of improper fractions requires having interiorized three levels of units — splitting alone is not sufficient.
Steffe, L. P., & Olive, J. (2010). Children's Fractional Knowledge. Springer. The reorganization hypothesis: children construct fractional knowledge through reorganization of their existing number sequences, not by learning a new kind of number from scratch. Olive (1999) documents how the explicitly nested number sequence (ENS) gets reorganized into a connected number sequence (CNS) when applied to continuous quantities. The CNS is the bridge between counting and fractions.
Vamvakoussi, X., & Vosniadou, S. (2004). Understanding the structure of the set of rational numbers: A conceptual change approach. Learning and Instruction, 14(5), 453-467. The framework theory approach describes fraction learning as a "slow, gradual process" constrained by the presupposition of discreteness. Children generate synthetic concepts — intermediate states bridging whole-number and rational-number perspectives. These misconceptions are not failures but visible traces of the reorganization process.

This is a Chomsky hierarchy boundary. Flat FSMs handle regular and context-free operations — the counting PDA, the COBO decomposer, the RMB compensator. These machines have fixed state sets and fixed-depth stacks. Fractions require context-sensitive coordination: three levels of units, a variable base, what the Prolog documentation calls "metamorphic accommodation."

The continuity argument (Steffe/Olive, Siegler, Boyce & Norton): three converging lines of research support that fractions emerge from whole-number crisis through reorganization, not categorical leap. Boyce & Norton (2016, JMB) confirm that the number of levels of units coordination students achieve in whole-number contexts predicts their fraction scheme construction. The transition is real but continuous: the learner's counting scheme cannot partition the unit, and it needs to reorganize so it can.
The "automaton IS the denominator" framing: a counting PDA parameterized by base-7 would treat 1/7 as its first tick, 3/7 as three ticks. The automaton itself is the denominator, understood as a recollected process. Common denominators then require synthesizing a meta-automaton that subsumes both base-7 and base-5 counting — the LCM as dialectical synthesis. Three incompatibilities documented in FRACTION_CRISIS_ASSESSMENT.md:

Variable base. The counting PDA is hardcoded for base-10. A fraction like 1/3 implies base-3 grouping. A fraction like 1/7 implies base-7. The base is not a parameter of the current machine — it is built into the transition table.

Three-level unit coordination. Whole-number arithmetic needs two levels (ones and composite units). Fractions need three: the unit, the composite, and the iterable composite. The current stack depth is insufficient.

Metamorphic accommodation. The strategy doesn't just need more states or a deeper stack. It needs to recursively compose strategies — a strategy that can modify its own transition table. That is not an FSM. That is something the formalization cannot currently represent.

The boundary is where the formalization honestly stops. And that honesty — that the system can mark its own limits — is the point of contact with the manuscript's central argument. The interesting thing is not what the system can do. It is where it stops and why.
