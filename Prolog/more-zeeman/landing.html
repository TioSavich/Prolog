What happens when a way of knowing stops working?

A computational agent starts with tally marks and nothing else. When it hits a problem it cannot solve, it enters crisis — and must either consult a teacher or build a new strategy from primitives. These three tools show the same process from different angles: the geometry of the snap, the formal dynamics of the Prolog, and the step-by-step narrative of what happened.

Four views of the same phenomenon

The More Machine

Feel the snap

A Zeeman catastrophe machine. Drag the blue point and watch continuous tension produce a discontinuous jump. The disc cannot transition smoothly — it must snap. The machine remembers each snap through Cantor's diagonal construction: read the history, negate it, produce something new.

The Bridge

Watch the formalization's internal dynamics

Connects the Prolog meta-interpreter to the catastrophe geometry. Run an arithmetic computation and watch the system's tension — inference count, crisis state, strategy transitions — drive the catastrophe machine in real time. When the learner hits crisis, the machine snaps.

The ORR Explorer

Read the story of learning

The Prolog server's built-in timeline. Shows the narrative of crisis-driven learning as a sequence of events: the attempt, the failure, the teacher consultation, the strategy synthesis, the retry. Each card records what happened at each stage of the ORR cycle — Observe, React, Reorganize.

The Playground

Grab a node, tug the springs

A coupled physics simulation where each state in a strategy automaton is a Zeeman catastrophe machine. Drag control points to build tension. When a node snaps, tension propagates through the network. Enough snaps trigger crisis — and a new strategy ring materializes from the inside out.

How they connect

Geometry ← Prolog tension → Narrative

The More Machine shows the topology of strategy transitions. Catastrophe theory describes how smooth changes in control parameters produce discontinuous jumps in behavior. You feel this directly — the disc resists, then gives way.

The Bridge maps the Prolog system's internal state onto that topology. The meta-interpreter's inference count becomes tension. Crisis classification becomes the control parameter. Strategy acquisition becomes the snap. The geometry is no longer abstract — it is driven by an actual computation trying to learn arithmetic.

The ORR Explorer shows the same process as a story. Where the Bridge gives you dynamics, the Explorer gives you reasons: why the system entered crisis, what the teacher provided, how the new strategy was synthesized, whether the retry succeeded. The three views are the same phenomenon — the geometry, the dynamics, and the account.

What this is

This is a research formalization, not a finished product. A Prolog system models how a learner acquires arithmetic strategies through crisis — starting from tally marks, consulting a teacher when stuck, synthesizing new operations from primitives. The model works for whole-number arithmetic. It breaks at fractions, where the representations become structurally incompatible. That breakdown is instructive.

The formalization is a tool for noticing where formalization stops. The interesting thing is not that the system succeeds at adding 7 + 3 through crisis-driven learning. The interesting thing is where the system's flat strategy synthesis cannot accommodate what a child actually does when encountering a fraction for the first time.
