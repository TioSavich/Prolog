We begin with NO! When a child, an adult, an animal, or a rock resists, it becomes knowable. The no is what separates — what makes something determinate. But the no does not last. The child is folded back into her parents' arms. We make mistakes. We repair them.

Critical Mathematics seeks to grow mathematical understanding through recognizing differences, acknowledging errors, and repairing them — over and over. From that beginning — which is not really a beginning, since every NO! arises from some unacceptable context — the project gets complicated. Follow your own no through this site and perhaps each complication will reveal itself as a fractal-like repetition of the first one. We say no, and then we say no to that no-saying. no. And then no to that. And so on.

A no arises, folds, and negates itself. What remains is a structure — small, inverted, insisting. The whole project is this structure at different scales.

This site is a set of tools. Each one lets you feel a moment of mathematical learning as it happens in a child — not as a formula to memorize, but as a crisis that a strategy meets, breaks against, and reassembles itself around. The tools are ramshackle. They are honestly so. They are also the clearest way I know to show what philosophy of mathematics, grounded in children's arithmetic, written from an anti-ideological position can look like when you make it do something.

Critical Mathematics formalizes the developmental sequence of children's arithmetic as a computational system where strategies emerge through perturbation and reorganization. The NO! is Norton & Steffe's perturbation — the moment a scheme fails at its limit and the learner must reorganize.

Each strategy documented by Carpenter, Fennema & Franke in Cognitively Guided Instruction is re-presented here as a finite-state automaton. The automata invoke one another as subroutines — counting-all inside counting-on, counting-on inside COBO, COBO inside RMB — producing the fractal nesting the manuscript calls metamorphic accommodation.

The interesting thing is not that the system computes 7 + 3. The interesting thing is where the representation fails — at fractions — and how it fails.

The NO! and its double negation are the observable trace of the dialectic the formalism tracks. When a strategy hits its inference ceiling, the meta-interpreter raises a crisis. The oracle provides a new strategy. The learner retries. The Polarized Modal Logic appendix tags the whole trajectory as compressive (the no gathering) and expansive (the no releasing into a new form). Every no is followed by a no, and the negation produces something genuinely new — a fresh row and column in Cantor's matrix, a fresh ring in the fractal nesting, a fresh state in the automaton.

The NO! is determinate negation. It is the Hegelian moment at which an implicit contradiction in experience becomes for consciousness. What is said no to is not annihilated — it is sublated, carried forward inverted, and re-contained at a higher level.

This project is a representation that can be made to behave like the reasoning strategies of children under controlled conditions — using Prolog, a sequent calculus with an erasure boundary, and a Polarized Modal Logic of subjective / objective / normative modes. It is not a proof that Hegel was right about mathematics. It is a tool for noticing exactly where formalization stops being able to say what a learner is doing — the arche-trace erasure points — and where a human judgment must enter. Those points are the content of the inquiry.

The no is written with strike-through because the negation is itself negated. Its form is carried forward; its content is cancelled. That is the sign of Derrida's arche-trace — a trace that is visible only as its own erasure.

The backbone of the site is a Prolog formalization. Its sequent calculus — proves/4 — models justifying; its meta-interpreter — solve/6 — models computing. They are deliberately kept apart because what the system can do is not the same as what it can give reasons for. The HTML pages are experiential companions — the geometry of the snap, the fractal of nested automata, the ORR narrative — each enacting what the Prolog formalizes. Where the companions work, they make the philosophical point viscerally. Where they break, they mark exactly the places the formalism was honestly never going to reach.

I.Follow your no through these⋆

The modules, each a no to the one before it

Each module reveals itself when the previous stops working. The modules nest — the next one does not replace the last, it wraps it.

00 · The More Machine embodied · proprioceptive
A Zeeman catastrophe machine. Continuous pressure produces a discontinuous snap. Model for the first NO! — the moment tension in the bands becomes untenable and the disc must jump.
● live · drag, or press "just watch" for autopilot
01 · Counting one, two, three… until it breaks
The developmental ground floor. Cost is linear: 99 + 99 is 198 steps. The strategy is correct — but correctness is not affordability. When cost exceeds what the meta-interpreter will tolerate, crisis is raised.
● live · try 7+5, 38+55, 99+99
02 · Addition COBO · RMB · Chunking · Rounding
The first shortcut: counting on by bases and then ones. Then rearranging to make bases (Hackenberg's slide). Each addition strategy is an automaton that calls counting as a subroutine.
● live · 4 strategies, each prolog-driven
03 · Subtraction take-away · missing-addend · sliding
Subtraction is not the opposite of addition — it is a family of different operations depending on whether the child is taking-away, counting the distance, or sliding both ends. Seven strategies, none reducible to the others.
● live · 7 strategies
04 · Multiplication coordinating two counts
Coordinating two counts. Distributive reasoning. The dialectic of part and whole where the part is itself a whole-of-ones. This is where unit coordination becomes explicit.
● live · 4 strategies
05 · Division dealing · measuring · inverse distribution
Division splits into partitive (how many in each group?) and quotative (how many groups?). The child's first intuition is usually dealing — the round-robin gesture. The other strategies derive from that gesture by cutting corners.
● live · 4 strategies
06 · The Fractal hermeneutic sphere through flatland
Each strategy is a ring; each ring invokes the one inside it. Imagine the hermeneutic circle as an action — an automaton whose q_start is also q_accept. Stack those circles into a sphere and drop the sphere through flatland. What you see at each moment is a circle. What's really there is the whole sphere — implicit, all at once, as the first NO!.
● rough · in redesign — the idea is what matters
07 · The Fraction Boundary where the formalism stops
The system represents whole numbers through crisis. It cannot represent fractions. That failure is not a bug — it is the point at which the flat-FSM architecture encounters what Norton and Steffe call metamorphic accommodation: not another strategy, but a new kind of strategy.
● open · research frontier — read the assessment
08 · Hermes the Hermeneutic Calculator
A neurosymbolic listener for math classrooms. The third presence on a Zoom call. Hermes hears teacher–student talk, codes it (TalkMoves · PML), maps it to strategies, anticipates moves, and quietly suggests pairings. Not a tutor — a listener. A separate product, born from this project.

II.Why this project exists⋆

Children invent strategies. They are not given addition; they figure out addition from counting, and counting from fingers, and fingers from the body. The mathematical content of schooling is, in one reading, an attempt to hand these inventions back to children as if they were ours.

This project puts the inventions back where they came from. Each module is grounded in something a child actually does, catalogued by researchers who sat with children and paid attention. Then the strategy is modelled — in Prolog, as an automaton — so that you can see what happens when it breaks.

Cognitively Guided Instruction (Carpenter, Fennema & Franke, 1999) documents children's strategies as they appear in classrooms. Steffe's constructivism (1992; with Olive, 2010) theorizes the schemes that produce them. This project takes the catalog and the theory and asks: if we model the strategies as automata that invoke each other, does the developmental sequence fall out as computational necessity? Sometimes yes. At fractions, explicitly no — and the no is productive.

Formalisms break productively — what the manuscript calls the Hegelian Infinite — when they are consistent under Brandom's (1994) interpretation of Kant's synthetic unity of apperception. The Prolog work is not a proof of that claim. It is a representation that can be made to behave like the relevant reasoning strategies under controlled conditions. Where the representation breaks is a site of productive contact with the manuscript's central argument. Fractions are the first such site. The erasure boundary in the sequent calculus is another.
