Understanding Mathematics as an
Emancipatory Discipline

A critical-theory approach to mathematical discourse — a 12-operator vocabulary for the epistemic posture behind what teachers and students say, and a pipeline from transcript to formalism to classroom.

Theodore M. Savich Indiana University working paper
I.The Problem⋆

What existing instruments miss

Students in mathematics-education courses are thoughtful people who are not talking to each other in ways commensurate with what they have to offer. When teachers elicit student thinking, they are doing something that feels collaborative but is often extractive — pulling ideas upward to be evaluated against unstated normative standards.

Existing instruments — TalkMoves, professional-noticing rubrics, the Five Practices — capture behavioral proxies for discourse quality. They identify what teachers do: press for accuracy, request evidence, revoice. But they miss the epistemic structure of what a speaker commits to about the relationship between their own knowing, a student's knowing, and mathematical norms. They see the move without seeing the consciousness behind it.

The behavioral code catches the utterance; it misses the posture. The posture is the content.
II.The Framework⋆

Polarized Modal Logic — 12 operators

PML is a 12-operator vocabulary for the modal structure of discourse. Three orthogonal dimensions combine:

Mode · Subjective (S)

First-person, embodied experience — what the speaker feels, proprioceptively.

amber

Mode · Objective (O)

The object as constituted for consciousness — what the mathematical claim, the histogram, the concept looks like.

rust

Mode · Normative (N)

Intersubjectively binding rules — what counts as valid. N mediates S and O.

teal

Modality · □ necessity vs ◇ possibility

A speaker who says "this is correct" operates in necessity (□) — a closed commitment. A speaker who says "this could be interesting" operates in possibility (◇) — an open orientation.

Polarity · ↓ compression vs ↑ expansion

The felt, temporal dimension. Compression (↓) is crystallization: contraction toward fixation, determination, the narrowing of a field. Expansion (↑) is liquefaction: release, integration, the opening of what was closed. Not metaphor — proprioceptively real. A teacher compressing feels grounded, certain, resolved. A teacher expanding feels curious, off-balance, open to what might come.

The 12 operators

□S↓FixationNarrowing of felt awareness into determination.
□S↑ReleaseThe inevitable softening after genuine letting-go.
◇S↓TemptationThe pull toward fixation; the lure of grasping.
◇S↑RecognitionRelease is possible — the choice point.
□O↓CrystallizeObject stabilizes as a bounded, determinate thing.
□O↑LiquefyObject dissolves into process, medium, multiplicity.
◇O↓EmergenceA pattern emerging from noise; coherence gathering.
◇O↑InstabilityInherent give sensed in what looked solid.
□N↓SolidifyNorms harden into rigid rules, fixed procedures.
□N↑LiquefyRigid norms dissolve into more universal, reflexive forms.
◇N↓New orthodoxyRisk that liberation hardens into a new rule.
◇N↑ContingencyAwareness that established norms are not absolute.

In practice, coders identify these postures through linguistic markers. Hedging ("I think," "maybe") signals ◇. Categorical assertion ("it doesn't work") signals □. Self-referential language signals S; talk about claims and evidence signals O; framework invocations and evaluative standards signal N. Whether the felt phenomenology and the discourse markers are reliably the same thing is what the reliability study will test.

III.Evidence⋆

What PML reveals — a concrete example

We coded a publicly available transcript from the TalkMoves dataset (Suresh et al., 2022) — a whole-class mathematics debrief already annotated with standard discourse codes. 24 turns. Both frameworks agree on most interactions, but they diverge sharply on 6 moments. The divergences are not errors — they are where PML identifies epistemically relevant structure that behavioral categories collapse.

Turn 06 · Teacher
"Ok. Um, what's the frequency for the bin that's the highest bar? I can't see it."
TalkMovesPress for Accuracy
PML◇O↓ + ◇S↑
The teacher asks for a specific datum (◇O↓), then discloses: "I can't see it." This is ◇S↑ — the choice point where she could pretend to know but instead occupies uncertainty. TalkMoves captures the function (eliciting speech) but misses the felt posture. The next student mirrors it: "According to me, it's approximately 42."
Turn 17 · Student S — rebuttal
"What I was thinking that it doesn't work because… it's real — it's more real to have 100 percent on histogram F while the lowest scores from 20 to 30, there's a bunch of people that has a low score."
TalkMovesProviding Evidence / Explaining Reasoning
PML◇S↓ → □N↓ → □O↓
Turn 21 · Student SN — counter-rebuttal
"I think that the situation you gave fits more to histogram A because histogram A shows no people getting scores of 70 to 80 or higher… whereas in histogram F a few people got a score that was 70 to 80, 80 to 90, 90 to 100."
TalkMovesProviding Evidence / Explaining Reasoning
PML◇S↓ → ◇N↓ → □O↓
Same TalkMoves code, but structurally different normative postures. Student S crystallizes a norm — "it doesn't work" (□N↓, the other interpretation necessarily fails). Student SN keeps the normative space fluid — "fits more to" (◇N↓, hers possibly fits better). One solidifies; the other negotiates. The distinction matters for how students learn what counts as mathematical argument.
Turn 24 · Teacher
"Oh, wow. Wow. That— I'm— Hey, you're not right or wrong answer. It's about the strength of your argument. I'm not— There's no right or wrong answer."
TalkMovesPress for Accuracy
PML◇S↑ → □N↑
The teacher's sentence breaks apart — "Oh, wow. That— I'm—" — consciousness expanding in real time (◇S↑), pre-reflective surprise. Then she commits: "It's about the strength of your argument." This is □N↑: normative liquefaction. The rigid norm (correctness) necessarily dissolves into a more universal one (argumentation). TalkMoves codes this as "Press for Accuracy." The teacher is explicitly rejecting accuracy as the standard.
Across the transcript a pattern emerges: the teacher exhibits what we tentatively call a decentering signature — ◇-dominant in N-mode, ◇S↑ present, □N↓ absent. If this pattern consistently appears in episodes experts judge as decentered, and is absent in episodes judged as centered, then PML captures something real.
IV.The Research Pipeline⋆

PML is one moment in a larger pipeline

…connecting critical theory, empirical data analysis, formal logic, and pedagogical intervention.

01
Data Cleaner
material subtraction
Strips curricular template material from student written reflections. Identify what the student was asked to say, remove it, isolate the reflective residue where identity-relevant discourse lives. Automated tagging preserves curriculum references so they can be studied separately.
02
Formalization · Prolog + LaTeX
where the representation breaks
PML provides a formal grammar. Prolog implements the 12 operators, proves internal consistency, and models children's arithmetic strategies as finite-state automata. The interesting part is not what the formalization captures — it's what breaks. Synthesis gaps, fraction crises, an arche-trace erasure boundary where formal proof goes hollow because subjective experience cannot be stabilized in the calculus. The breakages are not bugs — they are the places where formalization honestly encounters its limits.
03
Portfolio Tools
experiential, not theoretical
Interactive web tools that let educators experience the structures rather than read about them. The More Machine models tension and relaxation using Zeeman's catastrophe mathematics. The Hermeneutic Calculator makes visible how counting is a form of interpretation. You understand by interacting with the thing itself.
V.The Intervention⋆

Best Day — AI for human connection

The Best Day intervention tests whether this pipeline works with real students and teachers. An AI system analyzes instructor feedback on student writing, identifies complementary positions — students whose PML profiles suggest they are compressing where the other is expanding, or operating in S-mode where the other operates in O-mode — and generates dyadic discussion prompts tailored to specific pairs.

A pair might consist of one student who reasons algebraically and one who reasons geometrically. Rather than asking both to adopt the standard approach, the prompt invites them to explain their reasoning to each other in real time. The AI does not interact with students. The AI does not tell them which thinking is "right." The AI simply creates conditions for intersubjective recognition — moments where students encounter a genuinely different way of knowing and must negotiate what both ways are doing.

The contradiction — using AI to foster human connection — is the point. It expresses a conviction about what mathematical understanding actually is: not the internalization of norms, but the capacity to recognize the grounds on which different speakers are staking different claims about what counts as knowing.
VI.Next Steps⋆

Reliability and validation

If PML captures epistemic structure that matters for teaching and learning, it should be reliable and predictive. Four concurrent directions:

R1
Reliability study
Train 2–3 independent coders on PML, code a subset of the TalkMoves dataset (~120 minutes of transcript), and measure inter-rater agreement. If operators can be applied reliably, the framework is viable. If reliability is low, that tells us which operators need refinement — likely the □/◇ distinction, which requires the most interpretive judgment.
V1
Validation against outcomes
Access the NCTE corpus (1,660 classroom observations linked to student outcome data). Test whether teachers with ◇-dominant, N-mode decentering signatures show different student trajectories — growth, persistence, equity gaps — than teachers with □-dominant, S-mode signatures.
A1
AI-assisted coding at scale
Fine-tune a language model on hand-coded exemplars, deploy it to code large video transcripts automatically, compare against human codes. Methodologically controversial, practically necessary — human coding alone cannot reach the scale needed for robust comparison.
I1
Intervention measurement
Track whether teachers who use PML-informed materials in their own classrooms shift their modal profiles. Do they talk differently after encountering the framework? Does student discourse change in response?
