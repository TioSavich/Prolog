# What Listening Costs, and What Listening Knows

## A note on classroom monitoring as a pedagogical, not just technical, problem

Most ambient-listening tools that promise to help teachers monitor a math classroom do the harder version of the problem nobody actually needs to solve. They take a noisy room — overlapping voices, thirty utterances at once, the HVAC, a chair scraping — and try to recover clean speech from it, hoping that if the speech is clean enough some downstream model will know what to do with it. The signal processing alone consumes the whole engineering budget. By the time the audio is "clean," the moment is gone, and what comes back to the teacher is a transcript she could have read at the end of class anyway.

The mistake is treating the classroom as an under-constrained inverse problem. It isn't. A math classroom is one of the most heavily constrained discursive environments a person can construct. The teacher knows the standard. She knows the problem. She knows what a correct solution sounds like, what the three or four common wrong paths sound like, what the misconception phrases are — *cross-multiply because you always cross-multiply*, *the bigger denominator means the bigger fraction*, *just add a zero*. She knows which strategies the curriculum has put in front of these students and which it hasn't. The discursive grammar of the next ten minutes is mostly already specified. What the teacher does not know is which student, when, and in what way.

This is the same shape as the music transcription problem I have been thinking about for Hazy Hollows. There, the question is: can you turn a vocal performance into a score? The standard tools approach it as raw audio → notes, throwing away the lyrics and the chord chart even when they exist. But if you have the lyrics, you have a syllable grid; if you have the chord chart, you have a key and a small set of likely pitches at every moment. You are no longer doing pitch detection. You are doing *pitch selection* from a small candidate set at known time windows. The audio's job shrinks to disambiguating among options the structural data has already narrowed.

The classroom is the same architecture, inverted. In the music case, structural priors narrow the search so the system can commit to a transcription. In the classroom case, structural priors narrow the search so the system can recognize *deviation* — and deviation is the whole point. A student who is moving fluently through the expected strategy needs no monitoring; she is fine. A student whose talk pattern-matches a named misconception needs the teacher's eyes; the system can flag her by name, with the specific misconception named, because that misconception is in the symbolic backbone. A student whose talk fits no expected pattern at all — neither the strategy nor any of the catalogued misconceptions — is the rarest and most pedagogically interesting case: she may be confused in a way nobody has documented, or she may be doing something genuinely original. Either way, the teacher needs to know.

What I want to draw attention to is that this is a properly hermeneutic instrument. It does not "understand" the classroom. It *expects* the classroom, in the technical sense — it carries forward a horizon of anticipated meaning grounded in standards, problem text, and methods knowledge — and what counts as data is the gap between the anticipated and what arrives. The listening is structured by what the teacher already commits herself to as the lesson's possibility space. When a student says something the system can identify as Strategy B's third step, that is not really news; the teacher could have predicted it. When a student says something the system has to flag as "outside any known pattern," that is news, and it is news the teacher would have wanted. The instrument's value is precisely calibrated to what the teacher does not already know.

This inverts the usual framing of ed-tech listening tools, which sell themselves on coverage — "we transcribe everything, you decide what matters." The cost of that framing is that the teacher has to do the filtering, which she already does, in real time, with her ears, better than any model. What she cannot do is be in four conversations at once. A constrained-decoding system cannot be in four conversations at once either, but it can monitor four against a known grammar and surface only the residuals. It compresses the teacher's attention toward what her own pedagogical commitments have marked as worth attending to.

This also reframes what we are asking preservice teachers to develop. The act of building the symbolic backbone — what are the strategies students are likely to deploy on this task, what are the misconceptions you expect to hear, what does fluent versus shaky talk sound like in this domain — is not preparatory paperwork for the lesson. It is the lesson's listening apparatus. A teacher who has not built it is listening with no priors, which is to say she is doing the harder version of the problem nobody needs to solve. A teacher who has built it is listening with constraints, which means she can hear surprise.

The 5 Practices framework is already gesturing at this, though the language has not been technical: *anticipating* student responses is exactly the construction of a decoding grammar. *Monitoring* is the act of running the actual classroom signal against that grammar. What 5 Practices does not yet say — what the technical analogy makes visible — is that the quality of monitoring is bounded by the quality of anticipation. You can only hear surprise against a horizon of expectation. A teacher whose anticipation is thin will hear a thin classroom: most of what students say will land as undifferentiated noise, and she will respond to volume rather than content. A teacher whose anticipation is rich will hear a rich classroom: even the same audio, from the same students, parses into named moves and named misconceptions, with the genuinely novel utterances standing out clearly because everything else has been catalogued.

The pedagogical implication for E343 is that anticipation is not a planning task. It is the construction of the ear with which the lesson will be heard. We can teach this directly. We can hand preservice teachers a problem and ask not "how would you solve it" but "what are the seven things students could say while solving it, and which of those would tell you something you do not already know?" The exercise builds the listening grammar at the same time it builds the lesson plan, and it makes explicit what experienced teachers do tacitly: they walk into class already having narrowed the search space, so that the residual — the kid who said the surprising thing — actually surfaces.

The technical instrument I am building (Hermes) is, in that sense, a model of what a teacher's anticipatory horizon makes possible. It will fail in exactly the ways a teacher with thin anticipation fails: it will hear noise where a richer model would hear named misconceptions; it will miss novelty because it cannot tell novelty from mishearing. The instrument is not a substitute for the teacher's own listening. It is an externalization of one part of it — the catalogued part — so that the teacher's attention, which is the scarce resource, can go to the residuals the instrument flags rather than to the routine talk it absorbs.

This is what I want preservice teachers to take from the work: that listening is not an unstructured receptivity to whatever a student happens to say, but a disciplined practice of having committed yourself, in advance, to a grammar of possible meanings against which any actual utterance can register as fluent, as a known confusion, or as something genuinely new. The grammar comes from the curriculum, the methods literature, and the teacher's own experience. The act of putting it in place before the lesson starts is what makes the lesson hearable.
